# Financial Markets (2011), ECON 252 Lecture 7｜Yale ECON252（2011）第7講：有效市場假說、Sharpe ratio 與隨機漫步｜中英對照稿

## Chapter 1. Swensen's Lecture in Retrospect and Manipulations of the Sharpe Ratio｜第一章：回顧 Swensen 的演講與操弄 Sharpe ratio

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

And I want to talk today about efficient markets, which is a theory that is a half-truth, I will say. Before I start, I wanted to just give a few thoughts about David Swensen's lecture last period. Let me say, first of all, the Efficient Markets Hypothesis or the Efficient Markets Theory is a theory that markets efficiently incorporate all public information. And that, therefore, you cannot beat the market, because the market has all the information in it. You think you're smarter than the market, that you know something? No, the market knows more than you do. And you'll find out that the market wins every time. That's the Efficient Markets Hypothesis. So, it's a very far-reaching hypothesis. It means that, don't even try to beat the market. That was a very rudimentary introduction to today's lecture.

我今天想談的是有效市場（efficient markets），我會說，這是一套「半真半假」的理論。開始之前，我想先對上一堂 David Swensen 的演講補幾句話。首先，有效市場假說（Efficient Markets Hypothesis）或有效市場理論（Efficient Markets Theory）主張：市場會有效率地把所有公開資訊納入價格；因此你不可能打敗市場，因為市場裡已經包含所有資訊。你以為你比市場聰明、你握有什麼消息？不，市場知道的比你多；你會發現市場每次都贏。這就是有效市場假說。它的涵義非常廣：乾脆別試著打敗市場。以上是今天這講非常粗淺的開場。

But here I brought in David Swensen, who is claimed to have beaten the market consistently since 1985, and dramatically. And so, what do we make of that? That's the subject of today's lecture. By the way, after class one of you came up--thank you, it was nice, I don't know where you are--one of you came up and thanked Swensen for his scholarship at Yale. Yale now has need-blind admissions for the world. And so people, not just from the United States, people are helped out, so that people who have managed to meet the high admission standards here, makes it possible for them to actually come here. And that's substantially David Swensen who did that, because it's not just the generosity of the university. They have to have the money to do it. And so, somehow he seems to have made it.

但我在課堂上請來的 David Swensen，據說自 1985 年以來持續且大幅打敗市場。那我們該怎麼理解這件事？這正是今天這講的主題。順帶一提，下課後有位同學走過來——謝謝你，那很好，我不知道你現在在哪——有位同學向 Swensen 致謝，感謝他在 Yale 的獎學金捐助。Yale 現在對全世界採 need-blind 入學審查。因此不只是美國，各地的人都能獲得協助：凡能達到這裡很高錄取標準的人，就有機會真的來這裡念書。這在很大程度上是 David Swensen 促成的，因為那不僅是大學的慷慨——他們還必須有錢才能做到；而他似乎真的做到了。

Now, I know that there are still many cynics--the Efficient Markets Hypothesis has a lot of adherents still. And as I say, it's a half-truth. So, some people will say, well, Swensen was just lucky. And I say, how could he have been lucky for 25 years in a row? Well, not every single year, but pretty much. And they say, well, you're picking the one guy out of millions who is just the luckiest. So, those arguments are made. Anyway, one of you asked a question, which I thought it was very good, at the end. And that is why, in all of my discussion about Swensen and all of his talk he never mentioned the Sharpe ratio? Because as we said, the Sharpe ratio corrects for risk taking. That was one of our fundamental lessons. When we showed you the Efficient Portfolio Frontier, and the tangency line. You can get any expected return you want at the expense of higher uncertainty. You do a very risky portfolio, and you have high-expected return, because of the risk. If the risk is measured right.

我知道仍有不少懷論者——有效市場假說也仍有很多信徒。而我說過，它是半真半假。所以有人會說：Swensen 只是幸運。我會說：一個人怎可能連續 25 年都靠運氣？當然不是年年如此，但大致如此。他們又會說：你只是從百萬人裡挑出最幸運的那個。這類論證一直存在。總之，最後有位同學問了一個我覺得非常好的問題：為什麼在我們談 Swensen、以及他自己的談話裡，他從未提到 Sharpe ratio？因為我們說過，Sharpe ratio 會對承擔風險做調整。那是我們的基本教訓之一：當我們展示有效投資組合前沿（Efficient Portfolio Frontier）與切線（tangency line）時，你可以用更高的不確定性換取任何你想要的預期報酬；你做一個風險很高的投組，就會有很高的預期報酬——前提是風險要量得對。

But I think that's a good very good question. I caught myself not correcting for it, I just said Yale's portfolio had a high return. I didn't correct for standard deviation of return. So, David Swensen, in his answer, as you recall, essentially said he doesn't believe in Sharpe ratios, because we can't measure the standard deviation. The Sharpe ratio is the excess return of a portfolio over the market, divided by the standard deviation of the return. And that scales it down, so if the excess return is very high but also has a very high standard deviation, that shows they were just taking risks. And so the Sharpe ratio would reveal that. But Swensen said, I don't think that you can measure the standard deviation of return. Isn't that what is answer was? You were here. I may not be quoting him exactly right.

但我覺得這真的是個好問題。我也發現自己當時沒做這層校正：我只說 Yale 的投組報酬很高，卻沒有對報酬的標準差做校正。David Swensen 在回答時——你們若還記得——基本上說他不相信 Sharpe ratio，因為我們無法衡量標準差。Sharpe ratio 是投組相對市場的超額報酬，除以報酬的標準差；它把數字「縮放」下來，所以如果超額報酬很高、但標準差也很高，那就顯示他們只是在承擔風險；Sharpe ratio 會把這點揭露出來。但 Swensen 說：我不認為你量得到報酬的標準差。他的回答是不是這個意思？你們當時在場；我可能沒把他的話逐字引用得完全精準。

So, why wouldn't you be able to measure the standard deviation of returns? He gave a reason, which was that, well, when you're looking at a broad portfolio like Yale's, a lot of the things in there are private equity--that means privately held, so it's not traded on stock exchanges. Or it's real estate. Real estate is only traded every 10 years or 20 years, and so who knows what it's worth? All you have is an appraisal, but that's just some appraiser's estimate, so the standard deviation would be artificially low. He's right about that, but I think there's even more to that. I know there's more to this. There's a literature on this.

那為什麼可能量不到報酬的標準差？他給了一個理由：當你看像 Yale 這種很廣的投組時，裡面很多是 private equity——也就是未公開交易、不在證交所掛牌的部位；或者是房地產。房地產可能十年、二十年才交易一次，誰知道它值多少？你有的只是估價（appraisal），而那只是某個估價師的估計，所以標準差會被人為壓低。他這點說得對，但我認為還有更多層面；我知道這議題背後還有一整片文獻。

The point that I wanted to make is that you can do a--suppose you're managing money. And suppose the world out there is evaluating you by your Sharpe ratio. And suppose you have no ethics. You just want money. This isn't so obviously criminal, this is not criminal I suppose. So, you say, I just want to have the best Sharpe ratio for a number of years running. I'll get more and more people that will put money in my investment fund, and eventually I don't care what happens. I'll move to Brazil or something. Some foreign--pick out one. I want to get out of here with the money. So, all I have to do is fool people into thinking I have a high Sharpe ratio for a while. So, what do I do? Well, there's an interesting paper on this by--there's a lot of papers on this, but I'm going to cite one--by Professor Goetzmann and co-authors, here at Yale. It's actually Goetzmann, Ibbotson, Spiegel, and Welch. Maybe I'll put all their names on. Roger Ibbotson is a professor here, Matt Spiegel, and Ivo Welch.

我想強調的重點是：你可以——假設你在管錢；假設外界用 Sharpe ratio 來評估你；再假設你毫無道德，只想賺錢。這不見得是顯而易見的犯罪，我想也不算刑事犯罪。於是你說：我只要連續幾年把 Sharpe ratio 做到最好；就會有越來越多人把錢投進我的基金，最後我才不管後果如何。我會搬到 Brazil 之類的地方——隨便挑一個海外；我只想帶著錢離開。所以我只要騙大家一段時間，讓他們以為我的 Sharpe ratio 很高。那我該怎麼做？Yale 的 Goetzmann 教授與共同作者寫過一篇很有趣的論文——這類論文很多，但我先引用這一篇：Goetzmann、Ibbotson、Spiegel、Welch。我把名字都寫上：Roger Ibbotson 也是這裡的教授，還有 Matt Spiegel、Ivo Welch。

What they did is they calculated the optimal strategy for someone who wants to play games with a Sharpe ratio. So, you want to fool investors and get a spuriously high Sharpe ratio. And they found out what the optimal strategy is. And that is to sell off the tails of your distribution of returns. So, if your return distribution looks like this--this is return, OK? And you have a probability distribution; say a bell-shaped curve, OK? And so the mean and standard deviation of this would be the inputs to the Sharpe ratio. But if you're cynical and you want to play tricks, what you can do is sell the upper tail. These are very unlikely good events. Sell them and get money now, and then double up on the lower tail. So, you push the lower tail to something like that, and you wipe out the upper tail, so it goes like that, all right? So, that means you'll get money, because you sold the upper tail. You would do that by selling calls--we haven't talked about options yet--but you can do it by selling out-of-the-money calls. And you would do this by writing out-of-the-money puts.

他們計算了：若有人想在 Sharpe ratio 上「玩遊戲」——也就是想欺騙投資人、得到虛高的 Sharpe ratio——最優策略是什麼。結果是：把報酬分配（distribution）的兩端「尾巴」賣掉。假設你的報酬分配長這樣——這裡是報酬，好嗎？你有一個機率分配，例如鐘形曲線；它的平均數與標準差就是 Sharpe ratio 的輸入。但如果你很犬儒、想玩花招，你可以賣掉上尾：那些非常不可能發生的好事；把它們賣掉先拿到現金，然後在下尾加倍承擔。於是你把下尾推成那樣、把上尾抹掉，分配就變成那樣。意思是：你因為賣了上尾而先拿到錢。做法之一是賣出買權（calls）——我們還沒談選擇權——但你可以賣出價外（out-of-the-money）買權；也可以透過賣出價外賣權（puts）來做。

OK, but what you do it you make it, so that if there's really a bad year, it's going to be a doozer bad year for your investors. And if there's ever a good year, then, hey, you won't get it. But these good or bad years occur only infrequently. So, in the meantime, you're making profits from these sales and you have a high Sharpe ratio. But little do they know that you sold off the tails. And so, nothing happens for many years and you just look like the best guy there. So, it turns out that this is not just academic. There was a company called Integral Investment Management that did something like this strategy. It was a hedge fund. So, it was Integral Investment Management. It did something like this, by trading in options. And, it got lots of investors to put millions in them.

但這麼做的後果是：若真的遇到很糟的一年，對你的投資人會糟到極點；若遇到很好的大年，嘿，你也吃不到。只是這種大好大壞的年份不常發生。所以在此期間，你從這些賣出部位賺到利潤，Sharpe ratio 看起來很高；但他們不知道你已經把尾巴賣掉了。於是好多年什麼事都沒發生，你就像業界最厲害的人。這不只是學術：曾有一家叫 Integral Investment Management 的公司，做的就是類似策略；它是避險基金，透過交易選擇權做到類似效果，也讓很多投資人把數百萬美元投進去。

Notably, the Art Institute of Chicago put $43 million into this fund and its associated funds. And then, in 2001, when the market dropped a lot, Art Institute of Chicago was wiped out. They lost almost all of their $43 million. And so, they got really angry, and they sued this company. Because they said, you didn't tell us. What have you been doing? And then, the company pointed out, in its defense, that it actually said somewhere in the fine print, that if markets go down more than 30%, there would be a problem. And somehow, nobody at the Art Institute read that or figured it out. They thought the guy was a genius, because this company had the highest Sharpe ratio in the industry, all right? You see what they're doing? They're playing tricks. They're making it look like there's less risk than there really is. And there's a strategy to do that.

其中，Art Institute of Chicago 把 4300 萬美元投入這檔基金及其關聯基金。到了 2001 年市場大跌，Art Institute of Chicago 幾乎全滅——4300 萬美元幾乎賠光。他們非常憤怒，告了這家公司，說：你們沒告訴我們；你們到底做了什麼？公司辯稱：其實在細則某處寫過，若市場下跌超過 30% 就會有問題。不知為何 Art Institute 沒人讀到或弄懂；他們以為經理人是天才，因為這家公司的 Sharpe ratio 在業界最高。你看他們在做什麼？他們在玩花招：讓風險看起來比實際更小；而且確實有一套策略能做到。

It didn't end well for Integral Investment Management, because the Art Institute of Chicago managed to stick them on other things--they disclosed it. They told people that they were doing this strategy. The artists didn't figure it out. But there were other dishonesties that they nailed these guys on.

Integral Investment Management 最後下場不好，因為 Art Institute of Chicago 還用其他事由把他們咬住——他們其實有揭露：他們告訴人們自己在做這套策略；但館方（講者口頭上順口說成 artists）沒弄懂。不過還有其他不誠實行為，最後讓他們被逮個正著。

What Goetzmann and his co-authors did is, they showed that you can play tricks with the--you can play a lot of tricks in finance. But one of them is to play a trick with the Sharpe ratio. But their trick was very explicit. It involved particular portfolio composition involving options, and any professional would immediately know that that's a trick. And it didn't work for these guys; they were too aggressive in their manipulation. But you can do subtler things as a portfolio manager to get your Sharpe ratio up. Instead of manipulating with special derivatives positions, you could just buy companies that have large left tails. They have a small probability of massive losses, right? And you haven't done anything but pick a stock. And nobody knows whether it really has a small probability of massive losses, and you could systematically invest in that. And then you'd have a high Sharpe ratio for a while, and then you'd just blow up and lose everything eventually.

Goetzmann 與共同作者要展示的是：你可以在金融裡玩很多花招，其中一招就是操弄 Sharpe ratio。但他們論文中的花招非常明確：涉及特定選擇權投組構成，任何專業人士一看就知道是伎倆。對這家公司而言效果也不好：他們操弄得太過火。但你作為投組經理也可以用更細膩的手法把 Sharpe ratio 拉上去：不一定要用特殊衍生性部位，你也可以只買那些左尾很肥的公司——它們有很小機率出現巨大損失，對吧？而你什麼都沒做，只是選股票；沒人知道它是否真的只有很小機率會巨損；你可以系統性地投資這類標的。然後你會有一段時間 Sharpe ratio 很高，最後爆炸、把一切賠光。

I was thinking of an example from recent news. What about the strategy of investing in Egyptian companies that are tied to Mubarak? It might have looked very good for a long time, right? But the companies might have been underpriced, because people sensed there's some instability in Egypt. And look how fast it came on, right? I don't know what the outcome will be at this point, but it just happened--bang. That's a tail event, right? You might look at Egyptian securities and think everything is stable and fine, it's been 30 years, nothing has happened. But someone knows or suspects that there's something maybe unstable. And you, as an investor, wouldn't know that by looking at the numbers.

我想到一個來自最近新聞的例子：若你投資與 Mubarak 體制連動的 Egyptian 公司呢？很長一段時間看起來可能都很好，對吧？但公司可能被低估，因為人們感覺 Egypt 有些不穩；而你看它來得多快，對吧？我此刻不知道結局會如何，但它就是突然發生——砰。那是尾端事件（tail event），對吧？你看 Egyptian 證券可能覺得一切穩定、沒事，三十年都平靜無波；但有人知道或懷疑其中可能不穩。而你作為投資人，光看數字不會知道。

What I'm getting at is really what is the essence of Swensen's skill or contribution? It's not his ability to manipulate Sharpe ratios or numbers like that. I think what it has, to me, in my mind, is something to do with character and his real self and his real objectives. And this gets at what people in finance are really doing. I think that when you take a finance course in a university, you may not get a proper appreciation of how one, in a career in finance, develops a reputation for integrity. Nobody can really judge what you're doing as an investor, because they can't judge it from the statistics. Even though we've developed this nice theory about Sharpe ratios and the like, you end up judging the person and what the person's real objectives are. I'll probably come back to that theme again.

我真正想說的是：Swensen 的本事或貢獻本質是什麼？那不是操弄 Sharpe ratio 或類似數字的能力。在我心裡，它比較像品格、真實的自我、以及真實目標。這也觸及金融業的人到底在做什麼。我認為你在大學修金融課時，未必能充分體會：一個人在金融職涯中如何建立誠信的聲譽。沒有人真能從統計數字判斷你作為投資人在做什麼；即便我們已發展出 Sharpe ratio 之類漂亮理論，你最後仍會回到評判這個人、以及這個人的真實目標。這個主題我之後可能還會再回來談。

Let me also say that's about doing the Goetzmann--you understand the strategy? It's like investing in securities with time bombs in them. They're going to go off eventually, you don't know exactly when. And they look good for a while, but they'll blow up. What does the law say about this? Well, the law in the United States and other countries emphasizes that an investment manager must not fail to disclose relevant information about a security. And it has to be more than boilerplate disclosure. For, say Integral Investment Management, you could write up a prospectus and then say, but of course past returns are not a guide to the future and something could go wrong. That's a boilerplate disclosure, because people think, well, that's what everybody says. The law says that you have to actually actively disclose. If there's something that's relevant that would make your statistics misleading, you have to get their attention and explain it to them. That's the law. So, I think it's laws like that, and it's the development of--it's not something that we specialize in academia. Well, we are, we're trying to develop character I suppose. But it's not just Sharpe ratios. And, I think that tendencies to rely on numbers like this has led to errors in the past.

我也補一句：Goetzmann 那套——你懂那個策略吧？就像投資裡面埋了定時炸彈的證券：總有一天會爆，你不知道確切時間；它們一陣子看起來很好，但會炸。法律怎麼說？美國與其他國家的法律強調：投資經理人不得未揭露與證券相關的重要資訊；而且不能只是套話式揭露。以 Integral Investment Management 為例，你可以在公開說明書寫：當然過去報酬不保證未來、也可能出錯——那是套話，因為大家覺得每份文件都這樣寫。法律要求的是：你必須積極揭露；若有任何可能讓你的統計數字產生誤導的相關資訊，你必須引起對方注意並解釋清楚。這才是法律。所以我認為要靠這類法律，以及——學界並不專精這件事；好吧，我們也在試著培養品格我想。但不只是 Sharpe ratio；我認為過度依賴這類數字，過去曾導致錯誤。

## Chapter 2. History of the Efficient Markets Hypothesis｜第二章：有效市場假說的歷史

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

So, let me go more directly into today's lecture. It's about the Efficient Markets Hypothesis, which is, to me, a fascinating theory, which is not completely true, which makes it all the more interesting. The first statement that I could find--I'm interested in the history of thought--so, the first statement of the Efficient Markets Hypothesis that I could find was in a book by George -- George Gibson, who wrote a book in 1889 called The Stock Exchanges of London, Paris, and New York. And, he wrote, I'm quoting George Gibson, 1889: "When shares become publicly known in an open market, the value, which they acquire there, may be regarded as the judgment of the best intelligence concerning them."

So, they set up the Center for Research in Securities Prices at Chicago. 我補一點歷史：1960 年，University of Chicago 可說是先行者。Ford Foundation 撥款給 Chicago，要把自 1926 年以來所有股價資料彙整起來，而且要彙對。他們成立 Center for Research in Securities Prices（CRSP），拿 Ford Foundation 的錢到美國各交易所把資料收齊並校正。

## Chapter 4. Technical Analysis and the Head and Shoulders Pattern｜第四章：技術分析與頭肩頂型態

## Chapter 5. Random Walk vs. First-Order Autoregressive Process as Stock Price Model｜第五章：隨機漫步與一階自我迴歸：股價模型

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

Let me talk about Random Walk, which is a central idea in finance. The idea is that, if stock prices are really efficient, then any change from day-to-day has to be due only to news. And news is essentially unforecastable. Therefore, stock prices have to do a Random Walk through time. That means that any future movement in them is always unpredictable. The changes are totally random. So, the term Random Walk is an important term. It was coined not by a finance theorist, but by a statistician, Karl Pearson, writing in the scientific journal Nature in 1905. Now, he didn't link it to finance. But what he said is, the movements in some--well, he was thinking theoretically. Actually, I believe he used the example of a drunk. Let's take someone who is so drunk that each step is random. This person has no direction at all, staggering randomly, OK? So, he starts out at a lamp pole. And what would you predict--this is what Pearson asked--what would you predict is his position in 10 minutes? He happens to be at a lamp pole right now. And what Pearson said is, well, your best forecast is that he's right where he is now. Because you have no bias. He could go in any direction, equally likely. So, what's most likely? It's that he stays right where he is.

來談隨機漫步，它是金融的核心概念之一。想法是：若股價真的有效率，則日與日之間的變化只能來自新聞；而新聞基本上不可預測，因此股價在時間上必須做隨機漫步：未來任何走勢都不可預測，變化完全隨機。「隨機漫步」這個詞很重要；它不是金融理論家發明的，而是統計學家 Karl Pearson 1905 年在 Nature 提出。他當時沒連到金融；他用理論思考，我相信例子是醉漢：醉到每一步都隨機，毫無方向、亂晃。他從路燈桿出發。Pearson 問：你預測他十分鐘後在哪？他此刻剛好在燈桿旁。Pearson 說：你的最佳預測是他還在原處，因為沒有偏誤，各方向等機率，最可能就是他待在原地。

And what is the probability distribution? Well, it turns out that the standard deviation around that point goes up with √n steps, OK? Because each step is independent of the other, so the square root rule applies. So, if you're asked to forecast his position after an hour--that's a lot of steps--you would say, I predict he's right where he is now, but I now have a big standard deviation around it. Pearson's article is a very simple idea. Among the readers, apparently, was Albert Einstein and then Norbert Wiener, the mathematician who had invented a continuous version of the Random Walk, called the Wiener Process.

機率分配呢？結果是：圍繞該點的標準差隨步數 n 的平方根上升，因為每步獨立，平方根法則成立。若問一小時後——步數很多——你仍預測他在原處，但周圍的不確定性（標準差）很大。Pearson 的文章很簡單；讀者據說包括 Einstein 與數學家 Norbert Wiener，後者發明了隨機漫步的連續版本，稱為 Wiener process。

But it got into finance later. And in the Efficient Markets revolution, they started to realize that stock prices look more like more like a Random Walk than a Head and Shoulders. You look at these Head and Shoulders patterns and they're hard to find. So, what is a Random Walk? Let me just define it. A Random Walk is where you have a series in which the level equals the previous level plus unforecastable noise: \(x_t = x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t\), where \(\varepsilon_t\) is noise—mean zero and some standard deviation. Ideally, it would be normally distributed, so it would have a bell-shaped curve, and then the math would be very easy and very simple.

後來它進入金融。在有效市場革命中，人們開始意識到股價看起來更像隨機漫步，而不像頭肩頂；頭肩頂很難找。什麼是隨機漫步？我把它定義成：序列滿足「本期水準等於前期水準加上不可預測雜訊」，可寫成 \(x_t = x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t\)，其中 \(\varepsilon_t\) 是雜訊：平均數為零、有某個標準差。理想上它常態分配，數學就特別簡單。

So, I want I want to contrast that with an alternative, which is called a ''First-Order Autoregressive.'' Let me get my notation here. Let's take a process that starts at 100--that's like the lamppost. We'll say \(x_t = 100(1-\rho) + \rho x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t\). So, this is an AR-1. That's First-Order Autoregressive. It's like a regression model where \(100(1-\rho)\) is the constant term. And the coefficient of the lagged x is \(\rho\). And we usually require that \(\rho\) is between -1 and 1. Normally, \(\rho\) is positive. So, that means that it's mean reverting, but slowly. First of all, in the special case where \(\rho=1\), I wouldn't call it an AR-1 anymore, because it reduces to a Random Walk, right? If you make \(\rho\) 1, then the constant term drops out. I've got 100 minus 100--there's no constant term--and then I've got \(x_t = x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t\); that's a Random Walk, right?

我想把它跟另一個替代模型對照：一階自我迴歸（First-Order Autoregressive, AR(1)）。假設過程從 100 開始——像路燈桿。寫成 \(x_t = 100(1-\rho) + \rho x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t\)。這是 AR-1，像迴歸：常數項是 \(100(1-\rho)\)，落後項係數是 \(\rho\)；通常要求 \(\rho\) 在 -1 與 1 之間，一般 \(\rho\) 為正，表示均值回歸但可能很慢。特別地，若 \(\rho=1\)，我就不再叫它 AR-1，因為它化約成隨機漫步：常數項消失（100-100），得到 \(x_t = x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t\)，對吧？

重點是：人看股價圖會被騙，以為看到型態。粉紅線保證沒有型態，因為我生成的，只有隨機型態。但我看這條剛出現的粉紅線：看那上升趨勢，哇，這就叫牛市。我可以編各種理論解釋，但那些都是假理論，因為我知道真相：純隨機。我可以一直重做、永遠做下去。再一條：這條趨勢沒那麼正面。

Oh, actually, I have to say one thing, I forgot. I did put an up-trend in the Random Walk. I am sorry. In this simulation, I did add a constant, so that it pushed it up. 其實我忘了一件事：我在隨機漫步裡加了上升趨勢，抱歉。這次模擬我加了常數把它往上推；否則它就是隨機漫步。我剛忘了自己做了什麼：那是帶趨勢的隨機漫步。但它不保證上漲，因為仍是隨機。我再模擬一次——看現代電腦多快。若這是我們的歷史，人們會說二十世紀前半股市多驚人，我們會發明各種理論解釋，但其實是胡扯，只是隨機。我再多做幾次：看，整個二十世紀像個大隆丘，歷史學家會想破頭。這條往下，這是壞結果；Jeremy Siegel 不會喜歡，因為股市一百年幾乎沒賺。這些結果都同等可能。再看這條：若世界長這樣，我們會以為市場有線性趨勢，人們會建模說——看這條多直。

The point is that you start to see a sort of reality which is really just randomness. That's why Nassim Taleb, a friend of mine, wrote a book called Fooled by Randomness. 重點是你開始把「其實只是隨機」的東西看成某種現實。這也是為什麼我朋友 Nassim Taleb 寫了 Fooled by Randomness；我覺得書名與書都很棒。人不理解事物可以純隨機，大腦卻硬要賦予意義；你開始找型態，但型態可能毫無意義。我可以一直做下去，但先停。再看這條——當然我加的趨勢在幫忙。我想試 downtrend；因為加了趨勢，很難得到真正的下行。這條算有點下行：那裡有很多負向衝擊。

總之我現在要比較隨機漫步與 AR(1)。我做同樣的事，但改用——我知道我做了什麼，抱歉：粉紅線現在是 AR(1) 過程，所以變成均值回歸。我不是把兩者並排比較同一張圖，而是改用 AR(1) 重做一次演示。AR(1) 會想回到 100；我又加了趨勢，所以它其實回到線性上升趨勢。重點是它會比較貼著趨勢。我這裡示意的版本是圍繞 100（也可理解成圍繞趨勢）。若 \(\rho=1/2\)，表示若上期高於 100，本期仍高於 100，但只高出一半距離，逐步回到 100；下一步再縮一半，因此傾向回到趨勢。

所以根本差異在於：隨機漫步似乎比 AR(1) 更吻合資料。隨機漫步理論說股價不均值回歸：從今天往後全是隨機；若高於歷史趨勢——無意義，歷史趨勢是胡扯，只是隨機；忘掉趨勢、忘掉一切；永遠是路燈旁的醉漢，不論你站在歷史哪一點。但若 \(\rho\) 明顯小於 1，看起來就很不同，對吧？\(\rho=1/2\) 不是我們居住的世界；那樣賺錢太容易，趨勢周圍的小起伏都能套利。真實世界不是這樣。

但若真實世界是 AR(1) 且 \(\rho=0.99\) 呢？那跟隨機漫步差不了太多，對吧？很難分辨。在有效市場理論盛行的年代，大家對隨機漫步假說非常興奮；所以 Burton Malkiel 1973 年左右（緊接在 Fama 之後）出版的 A Random Walk Down Wall Street 成為巨銷書，賣超過百萬冊，因為當時人們覺得這是振奮的新智慧：我們學到股市是隨機漫步，並帶來各種引申。

The problem is – and I have to wrap up--that the Random Walk Hypothesis wasn't exactly right. It's sort of right, you've gotten some insights. But you know, maybe the real world is AR-1 with a \(\rho\) close to 1. And in that world, there are profit opportunities, but they take a long time to come. So, if the real world is AR-1 with \(\rho\) equal to 0.99 or 0.98, that means you can buy stocks when they're below trend. But then you have to wait 10, 20 years for them to get back to trend. 問題在於——我該收尾了——隨機漫步假說並不完全正確；它某種程度上對，你能得到洞見。但真實世界也許是 \(\rho\) 很接近 1 的 AR(1)；那個世界仍有套利空間，但要很久才實現。若 \(\rho=0.99\) 或 0.98，你可以在低於趨勢時買股，但要等 10、20 年才回到趨勢。這又像路燈旁的醉漢：原本是隨機漫步，現在我們在他腳踝套橡皮筋綁在燈桿上拉回來。若橡皮筋很鬆，他可以晃很久才被拉回，你不知何時；若很緊，就會明顯看到醉漢一直回到燈桿。

The problem is that the real world seems, maybe, to be more like the drunk with the loose elastic. And so, it's kind of unsatisfying. You can beat the market, but simple trading rules like Edwards and McGee are not powerful, short-run profit opportunities. In that sense, the Efficient Markets Hypothesis is right. So, don't forget the Efficient Markets Hypothesis. I'll repeat what I said at the beginning. It's a half-truth, it's half true. Remember that, but don't put too much faith in it, either.

問題是真實世界似乎更像鬆橡皮筋的醉漢，所以有點令人不滿足：你能打敗市場，但像 Edwards and McGee 那種簡單交易規則，並不是強而有力、短期的套利機會。在這個意義上，有效市場假說是對的。所以不要忘記有效市場假說。我重複開場說的：它是半真半假，一半為真。記住這點，但也不要過度迷信。
