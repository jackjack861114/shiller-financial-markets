# Financial Markets (2011), ECON 252 Lecture 21｜Yale ECON252（2011）第21講：交易所、經紀商、自營商與清算機構｜中英對照稿

## Chapter 1. Exchange as the Key Component of Economic Activity｜第一章：交換作為經濟活動的關鍵環節

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

Well, today I wanted to talk about exchanges and clearinghouses, primarily stock exchanges. So, these are places where shares in corporations are traded. And I think it's good to devote a whole session to them, because exchange is central to economics.

好的，今天我想談交易所與清算機構（clearinghouses），主要是股票交易所（stock exchanges）。這些地方交易公司股份。我覺得值得用一整堂課來談，因為「交換」（exchange）在經濟學裡非常核心。

In fact, I was struck recently. I was re-reading the presidential address of my old teacher. When I was an undergraduate at University of Michigan, I took a course by Professor Kenneth Boulding. I suppose I'm influenced by him. When he was elected president of the American Economic Association, he gave a talk about economics.

我最近很有感觸：我重讀了昔日老師的就職演說。我在 University of Michigan 讀大學時，修過 Kenneth Boulding 教授的課，我想我受他影響。當他當選 American Economic Association 主席時，他做了一篇關於經濟學的演講。

And he had an interesting definition of economics. What is economics? There's a lot of definitions given for the field, but one definition is the theory of the allocation of scarce resources. That's, by some definition, what the essence of economics is. But Boulding said, that doesn't sound right to him, because political science is about the allocation of scarce resources, and so is—even the family is an instrument of scarce resources. So, Boulding said in—it was his 1969 presidential address—that economics is the study of exchange. Obviously, it's prices and quantities that economics emphasizes, and those are parameters of an exchange. So let's say that equals economics.

他對「什麼是經濟學」給了一個有趣的定義。經濟學有很多定義，其中一個是「稀缺資源配置的理論」——有人說這就是經濟學的本質。但 Boulding 說，聽起來不對，因為政治學也講稀缺資源配置，家庭也是配置稀缺資源的機制。所以 Boulding 在——那是他 1969 年的主席演說——說經濟學是「交換的研究」。顯然經濟學強調價格與數量，而那都是交換的參數。我們不妨說：經濟學就等於這個。

I'm reminded of another book. I'm talking in very general terms first, and then I'm going to focus in on stock exchanges. But I'm reminded of another really important book, which I read so many years ago, by Karl Polanyi called The Great Transformation. And that was written in 1944. What is the great transformation? Well, for Polanyi, it's the invention of exchange. He said, what is the most important invention of man? Maybe it's exchange.

我又想到另一本書。我先講很一般性的概念，之後再聚焦股票交易所。我想起多年前讀過的一本非常重要的書：Karl Polanyi 的 *The Great Transformation*，寫於 1944。什麼是「大轉型」？對 Polanyi 而言，就是「交換」的發明。他說人類最重要的發明是什麼？也許就是交換。

According to his history of humankind, this was a relatively recent invention, Neolithic or more recent than that. He argues that in primitive societies, there is really no arm's length exchange. An arm's length exchange is one where you just quote a price and a quantity, and you don't have any other business. The price and the quantity sum it up. It's a business transaction. But Polanyi argued that until just something on the order of 10,000 years ago, there were no business transactions. There was only gift exchange. There were relationships people had. And you would solidify a relationship by making a gift to someone else, and then that person would later reciprocate, but there's no price, there's no exchange.

依他對人類史的敘述，這是相對晚近的發明，新石器時代或更晚。他主張在「原始」社會裡，其實沒有真正的「隔閡交易」（arm's length exchange）：那種交易就是你報價與數量，彼此沒有其他關係；價格與數量就把生意說完。但 Polanyi 認為直到大約一萬年前左右，都還沒有這種商業交易；只有禮物交換。人與人之間是關係網絡，你送禮給對方以鞏固關係，對方日後回禮，但沒有價格、沒有交換。

So, he claims that the development of our civilization is really the result of the development of the idea of exchange, and then an amplification of the idea as it became more and more pervasive.

所以他主張我們文明的發展，其實是「交換」觀念的發展，以及這觀念越來越普及之後的放大效應。

By the way, I've since learned—I thought Polanyi was very impressive—but some anthropologists question whether there wasn't exchange more than 10,000 years ago. And they cite evidence. One kind of evidence that's found of this is that certain commodities, even in the Paleolithic times, certain commodities are found far from where they were mined. Like flint to make stone tools, or ochre to make body paint. And they figured out where—you can do a chemical analysis and you can figure out where it was mined. And then, if you find that it arrived somewhere 1,000 miles away, there must have been exchange, right? The cave—no?

順帶一提，我後來知道——我覺得 Polanyi 很了不起——但有些人類學家質疑：一萬年前以前難道就沒有交換？他們提出證據。其中一類證據是：即使在舊石器時代，有些商品會出現在離產地很遠的地方，例如做石器的燧石（flint）、或畫身體彩繪的赭石（ochre）。你可以做化學分析找出開採地，若發現它出現在千里之外，那就一定有交換，對吧？洞穴——不是嗎？

**Student｜學生：**

Couldn't they have just killed someone?

會不會他們只是殺了某人搶來的？

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

OK. It could be that they just killed someone, right. So, I guess nobody knows.

對，也可能是他們殺了某人搶來的。所以我想沒人知道。

But anyway, some anthropologists argue that there was exchange. Maybe it was too pervasive. Of course, people did kill a lot of people in those days, too, anthropologists report.

但總之，有些人類學家主張當時就有交換。當然，人類學家也說那年代人們也殺了很多人。

So, maybe Polanyi had it exactly right. But I think he had it at least approximately right, that exchange has become a bigger and bigger part of our lives, and that's modern civilization.

所以 Polanyi 也許完全正確；但至少我認為他大致正確：交換在我們生活中占比越來越大，而那就是現代文明。

## Chapter 2. Brokers vs. Dealers｜第二章：經紀商（Broker）與自營商（Dealer）

I'm going to talk mostly about financial exchange. That's the subject of this course. But I can be a little bit more general. I wanted to start by distinguishing a broker and a dealer. What's the difference? It's a fundamental thing. A broker—actually, I've got this almost as a slogan—a broker acts on behalf of others as an agent to earn a commission. So, it's for others, trades for others as an agent for a commission. A commission is a fee. What is a dealer? A dealer trades for himself or herself, acting as a principal, not an agent, and profits from a markup.

我主要會談金融市場上的交換，那是本課主題。但我可以稍微一般化一點。我想先區分 broker 與 dealer。差別是什麼？這很根本。Broker——我幾乎可以當口號——代表他人、以代理人（agent）身分交易以賺取佣金（commission）。也就是為別人、替別人交易、拿佣金。佣金就是一種費用。那 dealer 呢？Dealer 為自己交易，以本人（principal）身分、不是代理人，靠加價（markup）獲利。

So, I can give you an example of each. When you buy or sell a house, do you get a real estate broker or a real estate dealer? That's almost obvious, right? Because you heard the term real estate broker so many times. When you buy or sell a house, you commission a broker, and you agree on a contract that pays the real estate broker a certain sum of money—maybe 6% of the value of the house—if a buyer is found. And then the broker doesn't buy your house, right? So the broker is an agent. And the 6% is the commission that the broker gets if he or she is successful in finding the other side of the deal.

我各舉一例。你買賣房屋時，你找的是 real estate broker 還是 real estate dealer？這幾乎很明顯，對吧？因為你聽過 real estate broker 太多次。買賣房屋時，你委託 broker，合約約定若找到買方就付經紀人一筆錢——可能是房價的 6%。經紀人並不會買下你的房子，對吧？所以經紀人是代理人。那 6% 就是經紀人成功撮合另一邊時拿到的佣金。

What's an example of a dealer? An antique dealer, right? Suppose you're buying a chest of drawers for your apartment. You go to an antique store, and there's someone there—your antique dealer—who has furniture that he now owns, having bought it, and makes a profit by selling it to you at a higher price, namely with a markup. He marks up the price that he paid for the item.

Dealer 的例子呢？Antique dealer，對吧？假設你要為公寓買一個抽屜櫃。你走進古董店，那裡有個人——你的 antique dealer——擁有那些家具（他先買進來），再以較高價格賣給你獲利，也就是加價。他在進價上加價賣出。

But let me just ask you, why is it that way? Why are antiques sold by dealers and real estate by brokers? I recently had a discussion with Guillermo Ordoñez, who is an assistant professor here, and we were wondering, maybe there are real estate dealers. Oliver helped us and found, actually, in Germany, a couple of real estate dealers. But only like a couple, and we couldn't find any in the U.S. And we had searched around in many other countries, and there was just virtually none, no real estate dealers.

但我想問：為什麼會這樣？為什麼古董由 dealer 賣、房地產卻由 broker？我最近和這裡的助理教授 Guillermo Ordoñez 討論，我們好奇會不會有 real estate dealer。Oliver 幫我們查，結果在 Germany 真的找到幾家，但只有幾家，美國則找不到。我們也在很多其他國家搜尋，幾乎沒有，沒有 real estate dealer。

Anyone have any idea why? I'm asking you to think. It's a little—or does anyone come from a country where they have real estate dealers? And that would end it, if you—I bet not, right?

有人知道為什麼嗎？我想請你想想。或者有人來自有 real estate dealer 的國家嗎？若真有，那就結束了——我賭沒有，對吧？

Well, what about antique brokers? Why not that? Have you ever heard of an antique broker? Maybe there are.

那 antique broker 呢？為什麼不是？你聽過 antique broker 嗎？也許有吧。

But it's an interesting question. It seems like some markets are naturally dealer markets, and some are broker markets. We thought of one explanation why there aren't real estate dealers, at least in the United States. We learned that a dealer has to pay income tax on the profits from a deal, and that is at a higher rate than a capital gains tax, and so that closes people out. You wouldn't want to be a real estate dealer because you end up paying a higher tax.

這是個有趣的問題。有些市場似乎天生是 dealer market，有些是 broker market。我們想到一個解釋：為什麼至少在美國沒有 real estate dealer。我們了解到 dealer 對交易利潤要繳所得稅，稅率比資本利得稅（capital gains tax）高，所以把人擋在外面。你不想當 real estate dealer，因為最後稅比較重。

The other thing is, I wonder if there's something about information, that someone who deals in real estate—it's just too hard to know what to pay for a house. It's so subjective. You could make big mistakes if you're going to buy houses and then resell them at a markup, because the market is just so variable, and it ultimately changes quickly. Maybe it's too risky. I don't know. I'm trying to think. But of course, there are real estate dealers, just very rare.

另一點是，我懷疑跟資訊有關——做房地產自營的人很難知道該為房子付多少。太主觀了。你若先買房子再加價轉賣，可能犯大錯，因為市場變異太大、變化又快。也許風險太高。我不確定，我在試著想。當然還是有 real estate dealer，只是非常稀少。

So, we're going to talk about stock markets. Which do you think stock markets are? Are they dealer markets or broker markets? Well, the answer is both. And now it's not as clear, and I'll come back to this. The New York Stock Exchange in New York is a broker market. Or they would say, an auction market. It's a continuous double auction market, where a broker facilitates the trades. The NASDAQ market is a dealer market. So, you pay commissions to your broker at the New York Stock Exchange; you pay a markup to your dealer at the NASDAQ.

接下來談股市。你覺得股市是哪一種？Dealer market 還是 broker market？答案是兩者皆是。現在界線沒那麼清楚，我稍後再回來談。New York Stock Exchange（NYSE）是 broker market，或他們會說是拍賣市場（auction market）：連續雙向拍賣，由 broker 促成交易。NASDAQ 則是 dealer market。所以在 NYSE 你付佣金給 broker；在 NASDAQ 你付加價給 dealer。

## Chapter 3. History of Stock Exchanges around the World｜第三章：世界各地證交所簡史

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

I thought I will start by talking about stock exchanges historically—and I have a lot to say. I can't cover it all; I was going to start with ancient Rome, I think I mentioned that before. The ancient Roman stock exchange is the first stock exchange that I think anyone knows about. But traders—I'm relying on the research of Ulrike Malmendier, who has studied the ancient Roman stock exchanges as much as can be studied. There's not that much evidence about it.

我想從歷史談股票交易所——我有很多可說，無法全講；我本來要從古羅馬談起，以前可能提過。古羅馬的股票交易所是我所知最早的證交所。但交易員——我依賴 Ulrike Malmendier 的研究，她盡可能研究了古羅馬證交所；證據其實不多。

But the traders met outdoors on the Roman Forum at the Temple of Castor. That's where you went to buy and sell shares. The shares in Latin were called partes—and the companies were called publicani. The peculiar nature of the ancient Roman corporations is that they sold—their customers were the government. And so they did services for the government, like provide horses for the army, or they would feed the geese on the Roman forum, on the Roman capital. They always fed the geese there, because the geese were hallowed in Ancient Rome, because they once warned of an invasion by cackling. So there was a publicanus that was in charge of feeding the geese. And also they would talk about share prices. It's known that they would go up and down even then, but there's no data on their share prices.

交易員在戶外、Roman Forum 的 Temple of Castor 聚會，在那裡買賣股份。拉丁文里股份叫 partes，公司叫 publicani。古羅馬公司奇特之處在於他們的客戶是政府，所以為政府服務，例如為軍隊提供馬匹，或在 Roman forum／Roman capital 餵鵝。他們一直餵鵝，因為古羅馬認為鵝神聖——牠們曾以叫聲示警入侵。所以有 publicanus 負責餵鵝。人們也會談股價；據說當時股價就會上上下下，但沒有數據。

It seemed that there was a long gap for stock exchanges after the fall of the Roman Empire, and the publicani disappeared. And there was a wide variety of financial arrangements, some of them resembling corporations. But the advent of the rebirth of the stock exchange didn't occur until 1602 in Amsterdam, when—I mentioned this before—the Dutch East India Company started trading.

羅馬帝國崩潰後，證交所似乎有很長的空窗，publicani 也消失。後來有各種金融安排，有些像公司。但證交所的「重生」要到 1602 年 Amsterdam——我以前提過——Dutch East India Company 開始交易。

And then, Jonathan's Coffee House—I like that story—in London. Lots of people would get together and talk there, and coffee houses became a big thing in the late 1600s. And somebody started posting stock prices on the wall at Jonathan's Coffee House by—what was the date—1698. And so the London Stock Exchange grew out of Jonathan's Coffee House.

然後是 London 的 Jonathan's Coffee House——我喜歡這個故事。很多人聚在那裡聊天，17 世紀末咖啡館很流行。有人在 1698 年左右開始在 Jonathan's Coffee House 牆上張貼股價。London Stock Exchange 就是從那裡長出來的。

And then, moving forward in time, now we're going to get lots of countries, but I'll mention the New York Stock Exchange. The traders of shares in the United States met outside, in 1792, under a buttonwood tree. I'll put buttonwood, it's a curious name. I think that's just a common tree that we still have around. And they signed the agreement to form the New York Stock Exchange.

再往後，會談到很多國家，但我先提 New York Stock Exchange。美國的股票交易員 1792 年在戶外、一棵 buttonwood 樹下聚會。Buttonwood 這名字挺怪，我想就是我們周遭還很常見的一種樹。他們簽約成立 New York Stock Exchange。

What else? Next, in my little history: India. By the 1850s, there were in Mumbai—or then called Bombay—traders under a famous banyan tree. They're all outdoors in Bombay. Banyan trees are more impressive than buttonwood trees. But it was by 1875 the Bombay Stock Exchange was founded. So that's the BSE. So that's been around for over 100 years.

還有呢？我的小歷史下一步：India。1850 年代在 Mumbai（當時叫 Bombay），交易員在一棵著名的 banyan 樹下，都在戶外。Banyan 比 buttonwood 壯觀。1875 年 Bombay Stock Exchange 成立，也就是 BSE，至今超過一百年。

But things have happened more recently. One thing that's been shaking things up—these are very venerable old institutions—what's been shaking things up is the advent of electronic trading. And these were kind of old-fashioned, venerable institutions. Do you know what happens at the New York Stock Exchange, what happened then and still happens today? There's a floor called the trading floor, and the various brokers meet there, just like in Jonathan's Coffee House. They actually physically come to the floor and they stand around, and there are posts for each stock. And if today you think you have a customer who wants to buy IBM stock, then you go over to the IBM crowd, and there's a crowd of brokers there who are trading. And you just do it verbally. You talk to them and you make a trade. That's really old-fashioned. There is something more electronic and more modern about the New York Stock Exchange, but that specialist post behavior still persists.

但近年有更多變化。這些機構非常古老可敬——真正撼動它們的是電子交易來臨。它們一度很老派。你知道 NYSE 發生什麼、而且今天仍部分如此嗎？有一層 trading floor，各種 broker 像 Jonathan's Coffee House 那樣聚在那裡。他們真的到場、站著，每檔股票有專屬 post。若你今天有客戶想買 IBM，你就走到 IBM 那一群人那裡，一群 broker 在交易。你口頭喊價成交。這很老派。NYSE 也有更電子、更現代的一面，但那種 specialist post 行為仍在。

Most of the world is switching over to electronic trading, and so things happen that—so, for example, in India they developed another stock exchange called the National Stock Exchange, and that was in 1992. And the National Stock Exchange was all-electronic, and so it was the modern version. It's rapidly gaining on the Bombay Stock Exchange.

世界多數轉向電子交易，因此例如 India 又成立了 National Stock Exchange（1992），全電子化，是現代版本，正在快速追趕 Bombay Stock Exchange。

So, let me go a little bit more forward. China, because of the communist government, did not have a stock exchange until 1990. And there were two stock exchanges founded in China in 1990—Shanghai, Shenzhen. And at least the Shanghai Stock Exchange is owned by the Chinese government, and that's why Laura Cha, when she talked to us about that—she was on the China Securities Regulatory Commission, which actually owned the Shanghai Stock Exchange.

再往後一點。China 因共產黨政府，1990 年前沒有證交所。1990 年成立兩家：Shanghai、Shenzhen。至少 Shanghai Stock Exchange 由政府持有，所以 Laura Cha 當時跟我們講——她在 China Securities Regulatory Commission，而該會實際上擁有 Shanghai Stock Exchange。

Oh, Latin America. Sao Paolo Stock Exchange, 1890. Mexico has only one stock exchange, but it was founded in 1894. So we seem to have like two different kinds of exchanges. We have the old exchanges, which are at least 100 years old, and we have the new electronic exchanges. Things have really sped up with the advent of electronic exchanges.

Latin America：Sao Paolo Stock Exchange，1890。Mexico 只有一家證交所，1894 年成立。所以我們似乎有兩大類：至少百年以上的老交易所，以及新的全電子交易所。電子交易所出現後，一切加速。

So, I mentioned the New York Stock Exchange, which started on the street outdoors and is an old-fashioned exchange that has been slow to update. But more recently, we have—I mentioned it already—NASDAQ. It stands for, originally, National Association of Securities Dealers Automatic Quotation System, which was created in the 1970s.

我提到過 NYSE：從街頭戶外開始，老派、更新慢。較近期有——我已提過——NASDAQ。原名 National Association of Securities Dealers Automatic Quotation System，1970 年代創立。

The interesting story about NASDAQ. In the '70s, the New York Stock Exchange was highly prestigious. It was the big board, the place. And a critical element of a stock exchange is that in order to get your stocks traded on the exchange, you have to satisfy listing requirements. So the New York Stock Exchange would examine any corporation that wanted to be listed on the exchange, and it was the prestigious big exchange, so it had high standards. So the company had to have a history of earnings, it had to have the right kind of management structure and board. A lot of things were checked out by the exchange, and as a result, the way it would work in the 1970s, a startup company could never get traded on the New York Stock Exchange. It would be traded instead by brokers off-exchange, or over-the-counter. So OTC means over-the-counter, that means not on an exchange.

NASDAQ 的故事很有趣。1970 年代 NYSE 聲望極高，是大盤、聖地。證交所的關鍵之一是掛牌要滿足 listing requirements。NYSE 會審查想掛牌的公司；它是聲望高的大所，標準也高：要有盈餘歷史、管理與董事會結構要對等等。結果在 1970 年代，新創公司永遠上不了 NYSE，只能由 broker 在場外交易，也就是 over-the-counter（OTC），意思是不在交易所內。

So, in the 1970s and earlier, the over-the-counter brokers would deal with each other, informally with telephone calls, or actually out on the street. Meet each other out on the sidewalk originally, and then they got telephones. And they had some record, which they called pink sheets, because they were traditionally printed on pink paper. These were lists of dealers' buy and sell quotes on prices of over-the-counter stocks.

1970 年代更早，OTC broker 彼此交易，非正式地用電話，或真的在街頭、人行道上碰面，後來才有電話。他們有種紀錄叫 pink sheets，因為傳統印在粉紅紙上：上面是 dealer 對 OTC 股票買賣報價的列表。

The National Association of Securities Dealers, then, was an organization of these over-the-counter traders. And in the early '70s, they set up the first computerized system. They decided that everyone's telephoning everybody—let's create a system that really works and that gets us the information. And so that was the NASDAQ system, the first computer-based system, which has now increasingly taken over much of the world.

National Association of Securities Dealers 就是這些 OTC 交易者的組織。1970 年代初他們建立第一套電腦化系統：大家互相打電話太亂——做一套真的有用、能把資訊集中起來的系統。那就是 NASDAQ，第一套電腦化系統，後來越來越主導世界很多地方。

I shouldn't imply that the New York Stock Exchange was entirely a laggard on this. Electronics played a role in stocks going back very early. The New York Stock Exchange used telegraph in the 19th century to convey prices, and they invented ticker tape machines. A ticker tape machine is an electronic printer that would print out stock prices. And in fact Thomas Edison, the inventor, his first invention was actually a ticker tape machine. That was in, I think, the 1870s. It printed stock prices. But all it was was a record of what had traded recently. It wasn't a system that helped you trade. You just reported what had happened; it was historical.

我不該暗示 NYSE 完全落後。股票很早就有電子元素。NYSE 19 世紀用電報傳價，並發明了 ticker tape machine：一種會印出股價的電子印表機。Thomas Edison 的第一項發明其實就是 ticker tape machine，我想是 1870 年代。它只印股價，只是最近成交的紀錄，不是幫你交易的系統；只是報告發生過的事，是歷史紀錄。

## Chapter 4. Market Orders, Limit Orders, and Stop Orders｜第四章：市價單、限價單與停損單

So, I wanted to show you what NASDAQ created in the '70s, and it's an order book that would be visible to everyone who trades on it. Prior to discussing that, I want to tell you about different kinds of orders. If you buy and sell stock, and you call up a broker and you say I want to buy and sell, the simplest kind of order is a market order. And you would specify the quantity. You would say I want to buy—and the name of the company, of course—I want to buy 100 shares of General Motors, or I want to sell 100 shares. But you don't name a price. You'll find out whatever the price was.

我想讓你看 NASDAQ 在 1970 年代創造了什麼：一本所有交易者都能看到的委託簿（order book）。在談那個之前，先講幾種委託單。你若買賣股票，打電話給 broker 說要買賣，最簡單的是市價單（market order）。你要指定數量：我要買——當然還有公司名——100 股 General Motors，或賣 100 股。但不指定價格；成交價到時才知道。

The broker will get you the best price—if he or she is a good broker, will try to get you the best price—but it'll still be unknown to you, because you didn't specify it. You might be unhappy with the price, OK? The price might be too high if you want to buy shares. And then the broker will say to you, well, if you're unhappy, you should've told me. You could have told me not to pay more than a certain amount for a buy, or not to take less than a certain amount for a sell.

好的 broker 會替你拿到最好價格——或盡力——但因為你沒指定，對你仍是未知。你可能不滿意價格，對吧？若你要買股，價格可能太高。broker 會說：你不滿意就該早講；你可以說買進時最高只付多少，或賣出時最低只接受多少。

So the alternative is a limit order. And so with a limit order you give both quantity and price. So if it's a buy, I want to buy so many shares but I don't want to pay more than such-and-such a price. Then the broker will keep that on his or her books until—well, whatever the agreement between you and broker is. It might expire after the day is over, or you could ask to have it kept on the book. And when the price becomes available, which is no higher than your specified price, then the order will be executed. Otherwise it won't be executed. And then for a sell order it would be the same thing. You specify both the quantity and a price, and the order will be filled or partly filled. Might not be able to get all of your quantity, but they'll fill as much as they can of it at that price or lower.

替代方案是限價單（limit order）：同時給數量與價格。若是買單：我要買這麼多股，但每股最高不超過某價。broker 會把單留在帳上直到——看你們約定——可能當日有效結束，或你可要求繼續掛簿。當市場出現不高於你指定價的成交機會，單子才會成交，否則不成交。賣單同理：指定數量與價格，可能全成或部分成交；未必能一次吃滿你的量，但會盡量在該價或更好（對賣方而言）成交。

And there's another kind of order, called a stop order. With a stop order you also specify quantity and price, but it's different. With a limit order, say it's a buy limit—well, let's talk about sell. If it's a sell limit order, you would sell the quantity at such-and-such a price or higher. With a stop order you would sell that quantity at such-and-such a price or lower. Let's make that clear. A stop order, also called a stop loss order, is an order that you can place with a broker to indicate that I'm worried that this stock might really collapse. I'm holding it, but I want you to sell it if the price starts falling a lot. So suppose the price is 100 today, I could put in a stop loss order at 80, and then at least I know I can't lose more than 20% of my investment, because the broker will immediately sell it when the price of that stock falls below 80.

還有一種叫停損單（stop order），也給數量與價格，但邏輯不同。限價賣單：在某某價「或更高」賣出該數量。停損賣單：在某某價「或更低」賣出該數量。講清楚：stop order 又稱 stop loss order，你告訴 broker：我擔心這檔股票可能崩跌；我還抱著，但若價格開始大跌請幫我賣。假設今天價格 100，你下 80 的停損賣單，至少你知道投資最多跌 20%，因為股價一跌破 80 broker 就會立刻賣出。

There's also a buy stop order, and that would be something that someone would rationally do if that person had shorted a stock. So if you had shorted a stock, and you were worried that the price would go up and ruin you, you can leave with your broker a buy stop order to buy the stock whenever the price exceeds a certain amount. And you would do that to prevent yourself from having unlimited losses on your short position. There's other kinds of orders, but those are the main kinds of orders.

也有買進停損單（buy stop order）：若你放空股票，理性上可能會用。你擔心價格上漲會打爆你，就留一張買進停損單：價格超過某水準就買回股票，以避免空頭部位無限損失。還有其他單型，但主要是這幾種。

Now, I wanted to talk quickly about limit orders—that's the most important kind. A lot of advisors say never place a market order. Why should you ever do a market order? There's always some price that you'd be unhappy with. You might as well say that. And so some exchanges don't even allow market orders.

我想快速談限價單——這最重要。很多顧問說永遠不要下市價單。何必下市價單？總有某個價格會讓你不滿，不如先講清楚。有些交易所甚至不允許市價單。

So, let's talk about limit orders. And what I wanted to show you was what a NASDAQ level II customer sees. NASDAQ is an organization—now it's a firm traded on its own stock exchange, it's called NASDAQ OMX—but if you want to subscribe to NASDAQ, it's very expensive, I understand. So you can subscribe at level I or level II, which is more expensive. I was going to show you an example of what you would see on your computer screen if you subscribe to NASDAQ level II for a particular stock.

所以我們談限價單。我想讓你看 NASDAQ level II 用戶看到什麼。NASDAQ 是個組織——現在是上市公司，叫 NASDAQ OMX——但訂閱據說非常貴。可訂 level I 或更貴的 level II。我本來要示範你訂 level II 時螢幕上某檔股票會看到什麼。

So this is a hypothetical limit order book for Microsoft. What it shows—this is not live, but it would be live on your screen, and these numbers would be changing before your eyes, flashing back and forth before your eyes. And so I've just frozen it at a moment in time. So what do we have? We have six columns here. This first column is the shares that people want to buy. So the first three columns correspond to those. "Bid" is the price there, bidding to buy these shares, OK?

這是 Microsoft 的假設性限價委託簿。這不是即時盤，但即時時數字會在你眼前變動閃爍。我這裡凍結在某一刻。我們有六欄。第一欄是想買的股數；前三欄對應買方。Bid 是出價買進的價格，了解？

Remember, NASDAQ is a dealer market. So these dealers are making these bids, or people are making them through a dealer, and MPID is the marketplace ID where these bids and offers are being made.

記住 NASDAQ 是 dealer market。這些 bid 是 dealer 掛的，或客戶透過 dealer 掛；MPID 是 marketplace ID，標示這些買賣報價在哪個市場。

So the first one shown, someone is offering to buy 100 shares of Microsoft at a price of $25.23, and that is listed on ARCX. ARCX, there's an interesting—I didn't mention that exchange. It's now part of the New York Stock Exchange. Maybe I'll come back to that in a minute, but let me just continue to explain this slide.

第一列顯示有人要以 $25.23 買 100 股 Microsoft，掛在 ARCX。ARCX 很有趣——我還沒提這家交易所；現在是 NYSE 的一部分。我可能等一下回來談，先繼續解釋這張投影片。

Someone called their broker and said I want to buy 100 shares, and the broker entered the bid through ARCX, and it's now on the NASDAQ screen. Someone else had a much bigger buy order, wants to buy 9,430 shares, and this came in directly to NASDAQ, but it's a penny less, $25.22. So you can just see what's going down.

你會注意到買價由高到低排列，越往下越低。電腦排序所有未成交委託。有人打給 broker 說要買 100 股，broker 透過 ARCX 掛進去，出現在 NASDAQ 螢幕。另一人買單大得多：9,430 股，直接進 NASDAQ，但價低一美分 $25.22。你可以看到結構。

另一邊——我們在這裡看到的清楚了嗎？螢幕另一邊是賣單，結構類似，但價格越往下越高，因為電腦反向排序。那代表各路人馬願意賣出的意願。例如有人願意在 $25.24 賣 2,400 股；其實是兩個客戶，先掛單的有優先權。另有人在 Cincinnati Exchange——我猜是那意思——同價賣 8,200 股，但列成另一筆委託，以此類推。

So now you're sitting here looking at the screen now, and you notice that this ask price here, it's higher than the bid price there. So what does that mean to you? It means there's no trade, right? Because someone is offering to sell at $25.24, and somebody's offering to buy at $25.23. It's no trade until somebody changes their price. That's no surprise, because these orders would be filled very quickly if there's a crossing. These two lines don't cross, so it's like if you plotted these curves, they're supply and demand curves, right? We could plot the amount at various prices.

你坐在螢幕前，注意到這邊的 ask 比那邊的 bid 高。代表什麼？代表還沒成交，對吧？因為有人願意在 $25.24 賣，有人只願意 $25.23 買。除非有人改價，否則不成交。不意外：若買賣價交叉，這些單會很快成交。兩條「線」沒交叉，就像你畫供需曲線——我們可以畫各價位的數量。

Well, you can see I'd have curves, a supply and demand curve that don't cross. Normally they have to cross somewhere, and then there's a market-clearing price. These things normally don't cross because if they did cross they'd immediately disappear from the screen. Someone would finish the order and it'd sell. But you, sitting at the screen, now have a pretty good idea what the price is.

你會得到不相交的供需曲線。正常情況它們要在某處相交才有市場出清價。這裡通常不相交，因為一旦交叉就會立刻從螢幕消失——有人把單吃完成交。但你坐在螢幕前，仍能對價格有相當把握。

A NASDAQ level II is better than a NASDAQ level I, because level I just gives you the first row. It's cheaper to subscribe to that. What NASDAQ level I gives you is the inside spread. NASDAQ level II 比 level I 好，因為 level I 只給你第一列，訂閱較便宜。level I 給你內盤價差（inside spread）：會告訴你 $25.23 有 100 股買、$25.24 有賣，你若要吃單可以任選一邊。但它不給全景。level II 讓你更懂市場深度；你若要玩交易遊戲會想知道。例如你知道價格可能很難快速跌破 $25.22，因為下面有大買單，要跌破會比較難。

若你在真實盤上看到這畫面，數字會狂跳。20 秒前的掛單可能一閃就不見。要成交就得動很快。

## Chapter 5. The Growing Importance of Electronic Trading｜第五章：電子交易日益重要

On a fully automated system the trades would be executed automatically, and this is becoming—electronic trading is taking over the world. And the orders can be executed by computers that make it instant, so that the number doesn't even appear on the screen long enough for you to see it.

在完全自動化系統上，成交會自動執行——電子交易正在接管世界。委託可由電腦瞬間撮合，數字可能快到你在螢幕上都看不見。

So one development that's coming in now is what's called high frequency trading, or HFT, which is trading that is done by computers. Once you have a system like this—when you have to trade through a floor broker on the New York Stock Exchange it has to proceed at human pace, right? The way it works is you make a telephone call to your broker, your broker makes another telephone call to the representative on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange, that person walks over to the crowd, and then discusses it, and indicates what—it's like a poker game. You don't want to reveal your hand, but you kind of feel people out, and then after a little discussion you reach a trade. But when you have something that you can hit on a computer it just goes instantly. So people start programming trading, and that's been an important phenomenon, because you see these moving faster than you can—these prices disappear and reappear so fast that you can't quite know, you can't act fast enough.

現在進來的一個發展叫高頻交易（high frequency trading, HFT），由電腦執行。一旦你有這種系統——若你必須透過 NYSE 場內 broker 交易，节奏就是人類速度：你打電話給 broker，broker 再打給場內代表，代表走到人群裡談判，像撲克：不想露底牌，但要試探，最後談成。但你在電腦上一按就瞬間成交。於是人們開始程式化交易；這很重要，因為價格變動快到你看不清、來不及反應。

So we have algorithmic trading, or program trading. So that goes back practically to the 1970s. Certainly by the '80s program trading was becoming a big and important phenomenon, and it's becoming increasingly important now. High frequency trading now—brokers will invoke what are called "millisecond strategies." You can actually flash an order on some of the exchanges that lasts a thousandth of a second. You can put a buy order or a sell order and retract it in a millisecond. This could be a trading strategy which you might employ. You could do that to discourage people from trading. If you want to trade only with the computers, if you think people are too smart for me, I don't want to trade with people, I want to rip off the computers, then you write a millisecond trading strategy, and then you can sort into who trades with you.

於是我們有演算法交易（algorithmic trading）或程式交易（program trading），幾乎可追溯到 1970 年代；1980 年代 certainly 變大，現在越來越重要。高頻交易如今——broker 會談「毫秒策略」（millisecond strategies）。你在某些交易所可以把委託「閃現」千分之一秒：下買或賣單再在毫秒內撤回。這可以是策略：用來嚇阻別人交易。若你只想跟電腦交易——覺得人太聰明不想跟人對做、想「對電腦下手」——你就寫毫秒策略，藉此篩選誰會跟你成交。

Now the interesting thing about millisecond trading is that it's favoring the electronic exchanges. As time goes on people are getting more and more sophisticated about high frequency trading, and so they want to trade on exchanges that are fully electronic so they can play all of these games. And that means that the floor exchanges are dying out over most of the world.

毫秒交易有趣的是它有利於全電子交易所。人們對 HFT 越來越精，想在全電子交易所玩這些遊戲。意思是場內交易所在世界多數地方正在式微。

By the way, the New York Stock Exchange—I was going to give you a history of this—the New York Stock Exchange has been slow to adapt to these technologies. Let me just give you a little history of—electronic trading is an exciting thing for many people, but I think it started—or the really interesting electronic trading started with the ECNs, electronic communication networks, that were allowed by the Securities and Exchange Commission as alternatives to stock exchanges. Stock exchanges are highly regulated by governments around the world, but in the 1990s the Securities and Exchange Commission allowed more sophisticated electronic trading, at least as an experiment. So they didn't call these things exchanges, they called them ECNs.

順帶一提，NYSE——我本來要講這段歷史——適應這些科技較慢。電子交易對很多人很刺激；我認為真正有趣的電子交易始於 ECN（electronic communication networks），由 SEC 核准作為證交所的替代。各國證交所受政府高度監管，但 1990 年代 SEC 允許更精密的電子交易，至少作為實驗。所以他們不稱這些為 exchanges，而稱 ECN。

One of the most important ECNs was a company called Archipelago. Another one was called Island. And these were actually just websites where you could trade, and they were open to the public. They had a different culture. They had more of a web culture. The web culture is we're not going to charge you to see the order book, we'll just put it out to everybody. The web doesn't charge you for a lot of things. And so they became popular trading sites for the general public.

最重要的 ECN 之一是 Archipelago，另一個叫 Island。它們基本上就是可交易的網站，對大眾開放，文化不同，比較像 web 文化：我們不跟你收費才讓你看委託簿，直接公開給大家。網路很多東西不收費。所以它們成為散戶熱門交易站。

They grew up the way the personal computer grew, so the New York Stock Exchange, when they first saw Archipelago, they said oh this is a bunch of college kids fooling around, some computer game, sort of. 它們像個人電腦一樣長大。NYSE 一開始看到 Archipelago 說：哦，一群大學生玩電腦遊戲吧。不認真。但最後不得不認真，因為 Archipelago 長太快。最後 NYSE 與 Archipelago 合併。現在——我想他們多數成交走 ARCX？我不確定是否「多數」，但至少很大比例走 ARCX。NYSE 在 2005 年買下 Archipelago；當時 ARCX 成交量已緊咬 NYSE。

證交所變化很快，因為科技在變。以前 NYSE 是單一聲望交易所，150 多年沒有實質改變；現在電子交易進來，一切被撼動。

So the New York Stock Exchange merged with Archipelago in 2005, and then they did another merger—let's say this—New York Stock Exchange with Euronext, which is another exchange in Europe, in 2006. NYSE 2005 年與 Archipelago 合併後，又與歐洲的 Euronext 合併（2006）。此刻（2011）他們似乎正與 Deutsche Börse 進行另一次合併，尚未定案。NASDAQ 也加入競逐，對 NYSE 出價；Intercontinental Exchange 亦然。（以上事件敘述截至 2011 年 4 月 13 日。）

我最終破產的機率是多少？我不推導了，其實不難推。

Here's the standard result in the simplest symmetric setup against an effectively unlimited counterparty: if my probability of winning on each bet is 1/2 or less, the probability that I am eventually ruined is 1. 標準敘述（最簡單、對手資金可視為無限時）如下：若每注贏的機率 p 不大於 1/2，最終破產機率為 1。若 p 大於 1/2，破產機率會低於 1——仍不會是零，但隨起始資金增加而下降。（講堂口誤處已順稿：逐字稿先出現「if p is greater than 1/2」再接 p = 1/2，與定理敘述不一致。）
