# Financial Markets (2011), ECON 252 Lecture 4｜Yale ECON252（2011）第4講：投資組合分散與支撐性金融機構｜中英對照稿

## Chapter 1. Introduction｜第一章：導論

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

OK, so this is the fourth lecture for Economics 252, Financial Markets. And I wanted today to talk about some really basic concepts, about portfolios. A portfolio is a collection of investments. And I want to talk about risk and return, and eventually get into the core theory, which is the Capital Asset Pricing Model, in finance.

好的，這是經濟學 252「金融市場」的第四講。我今天想談一些非常基礎的概念，關於投資組合（portfolio）。投資組合就是一系列投資的集合。我想談風險與報酬，最後進到金融學的核心理論之一：資本資產定價模型（Capital Asset Pricing Model, CAPM）。

But first I wanted to say something about last lecture. Last time, I talked about innovation in finance. And I presented finance as a sort of branch of engineering in a way. We invent financial devices. And the devices serve certain functions, and, in order to serve those functions, they have a number of details that have to be gotten right. Moreover, there's a process of invention, and the process of invention involves experimentation, and when an experiment doesn't work, we forget about it and we move on, but when it does work it gets copied all over the world.

但在那之前，我想先回顧上一講。上次我談到金融創新。我把金融在某種意義上呈現為工程的一支：我們發明金融工具（financial devices）。這些工具要服務特定功能，而要服務那些功能，就有許多細節必須做對。此外，發明是一個過程，過程裡包含實驗；實驗若不成功，我們就忘掉它並往前走；但若成功，它就會被複製到全世界。

## Chapter 2. United East India Company and Amsterdam Stock Exchange｜第二章：United East India Company 與 Amsterdam Stock Exchange

So, I thought a nice way to transition to today's lecture would be to talk about one very important moment in the history of finance: when the first real important stock was invented. And it was, see if I can spell it right, Vereenigde Oost-Indische, I might be misspelling this, Compagnie 1602. This was the first--Did I get that all right? I think got it right! That's Dutch for the United East India Company. It was founded in that year. It was a time, when Holland was at war, and the government was worried about the economy and willing to experiment with raising capital to keep the economy prospering. And someone had this idea. Let's start a company with shares in it, and let's trade them.

所以我覺得，銜接到今天這講的一個好方式，是談金融史上非常重要的一刻：第一檔真正重要的股票被發明出來。它叫做——看我拼對沒有——Vereenigde Oost-Indische（我可能拼錯）Compagnie，1602。這是第一……我拼對了嗎？我想對了！那是荷蘭文的 United East India Company。它在這一年成立。當時 Holland 在打仗，政府擔心經濟，也願意實驗如何籌資以維持繁榮。於是有人提出想法：我們成立一家有股份的公司，然後交易那些股份。

And in the same year, and I can't write this in Dutch, but they created the Amsterdam Stock Exchange. And initially it had only one stock. And so this is called VOC. OK? And it was a trading company. They were going to set them up, and they were going to buy ships, and they're going to sail all over the world, and they were going to trade in various commodities. So, it sounds pretty basic. But no one had ever done this before. So, it's interesting how much got invented in this one year, 1602.

同年——我不會用荷蘭文寫——他們創立了 Amsterdam Stock Exchange。一開始它只有一檔股票。這家公司就叫 VOC。它是一家貿易公司：他們要造船、航行世界各地、交易各種商品。聽起來很基本，但以前從沒人這樣做過。所以很有趣：1602 這一年竟然一次發明了這麼多東西。

First of all, they invented a corporate logo. I don't know if I have it right. It was something like V-O-C, I don't know if I did that right, just like we would put on, you know, advertisements for a company today. Maybe that's not very important. But what's really important, also, is that this was a long-term venture. There were lots of ventures already in Europe, where a group of merchants would get together and they would pool their money for one trip. They would send ships out, and these ships would trade and come back, and then they'd dissolved the whole thing. But this was different. This one was going to go indefinitely. And in their initial announcement, they said, we're going to set up operations all over the world. We're going to have an office in India, and another one, I don't know, in Indonesia, and, it's a big thing. And in the New World, in America, but primarily East Indies, from the name.

首先，他們發明了公司標誌（corporate logo）。我不確定我講得對不對，大概是 V-O-C 之類，就像今天公司廣告會放的東西。那可能不是最重要。更重要的是，這是一個長期事業。歐洲當時已有很多「單趟」合資：商人湊錢做一趟航行，船回來就整個解散。但這次不同：這次要無限期經營下去。他們在最初公告說：我們要在全世界設點，India 要有辦公室，Indonesia 也要有，規模很大；New World、America 也會有，但主要仍是 East Indies——從名字看得出來。

But the interesting thing is they set up a stock exchange to trade shares in it. And the stock exchange arranged that you could trade every day. So, there was lively trading. This was part of the idea. Because when they set up a company in those days, you could get your shares when they founded the company, and that was it, right? I mean, you couldn't trade them, or maybe you could infrequently. The company might open its books once a year, and they would take new shareholders in. But someone had--hey, this is an idea. Even though the VOC doesn't open its books regularly, we can trade them every day. What difference does it make? You know, so we're going to have stockbrokers on the Amsterdam stock exchange. And maybe they'll own some shares in VOC, OK? And then they'll have an inventory of shares. And then somebody wants to buy some, you buy them from the broker. You don't have to contact the company. And then the broker will, you know, maybe at the year-end, will report to the company that you own the shares. But doesn't have to, right? The broker does it. All right.

但真正有趣的是：他們設立證券交易所來交易它的股份。交易所安排你可以每天交易，所以交易很熱絡，這也是整套想法的一部分。因為當時成立公司，你通常只能在創立時拿到股份，然後就結束了——不能交易，或只能很不常地交易。公司可能一年開一次帳接新股東。但有人想到：嘿，就算 VOC 不常開帳，我們也可以每天交易啊，有什麼差別？所以在 Amsterdam stock exchange 會有股票經紀人（stockbrokers）。他們可能自己持有一些 VOC 股份，當作庫存；有人想買，就從經紀人那裡買，你不必聯絡公司。經紀人可能年底才向公司報告你持有股份，但其實也不必，對吧？經紀人處理就好。

So, the broker says you own these shares. You trust the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, because they have rules and code of ethics, so you think you own, well, you do own VOC shares if you buy them from a broker. The VOC doesn't know it yet, but you've got the shares, because the broker is a member of the Amsterdam Stock Exchange and says that you have the shares. Then, almost immediately after 1602, a funny thing happened. Can you guess what it was? The brokers started selling more shares than they had, right? What's to stop them from doing that? Or some of them did that, right? So, the broker maybe owns some shares in VOC and he gets lots of buyers and the broker ends up selling more, more shares than he has. And he thinks, well, I'll get them later, you know. And he says, what do my customers care, if they own shares, because I don't report to the company right away anyway. I'll make good on this. I'm a broker. I know what to do. I'll buy them later and I'll get them.

所以經紀人說你擁有這些股份。你相信 Amsterdam Stock Exchange，因為它有規則與道德準則，所以你認為——你也確實擁有——若你向經紀人買了 VOC 股份。VOC 可能還不知道，但你已經持有，因為經紀人是交易所會員，他說你有就有。然後 1602 之後幾乎立刻發生一件有趣的事：你猜是什麼？經紀人開始賣出比他實際擁有更多的股份，對吧？有什麼能阻止？或至少有些人這樣做。經紀人可能持有一些 VOC，但買家太多，結果賣出超過他擁有的數量。他想：我晚點再補貨就好。他又想：客戶在乎什麼？反正我不會立刻向公司報告；我會履約，我是經紀人，我知道怎麼做，我晚點買回來補上。

So, you see what starts to happen? There are more shares out there being traded than there exists in the company, because the broker is selling shares that he doesn't own. And so there began in, way back, even in this time, there began what we call short-sales, or short-interest. It happens when you set up a stock market and you allow, well, we would call it today street name, owning a stock in street name. We have stock exchanges, many stock exchanges in the world today, and they, including--by the way, the Amsterdam Stock Exchange is the oldest stock exchange in the world and it's still trading. But it has merged. First, it merged with Brussels, and Paris, and now they're called Euronext Amsterdam. But they're still doing this. Nothing stopped them in over 400 years. They keep doing it.

你看到會發生什麼：市場上被交易的股份數，可能超過公司實際發行量，因為經紀人在賣他不擁有的股份。於是很早、早在當時，就出現了我們今天所謂的 short-sales 或 short-interest。當你設立股票市場並允許——我們今天稱為 street name——以街名持有股票時，就會發生。今天世界上很多證券交易所都如此；順帶一提，Amsterdam Stock Exchange 是世界上最古老且仍在交易的交易所之一。它後來合併：先與 Brussels、Paris，現在叫 Euronext Amsterdam。但他們仍在做同樣的事：400 多年沒停下來。

But this stock exchange, and many others like it, allows brokers to sell you stocks in what's called street name, OK? And what that means is that when you buy shares, the broker puts in your account that you own these shares, but the company doesn't know it, because the actual ownership is registered in the name of the broker, OK. And so, the broker is selling you shares. And it's only through the broker that you know that you have shares. So, the broker on the stock exchange may be short. May have sold more shares then he or she has. That's all right, OK? It happened as long ago as right from the beginning of the stock market.

但這個交易所（以及許多類似者）允許經紀人以 street name 賣股票給你。意思是：你買股時，經紀人在你帳上記錄你擁有股份，但公司不知道，因為法定所有權登記在經紀人名下。經紀人賣股份給你；你也只能透過經紀人知道你持有股份。所以交易所上的經紀人可能是 short 的：賣出多於他／她實際擁有的數量。這沒關係，對吧？從股票市場一開始就是如此。

There was a scandal. I was reading the history of this, and, who was the guy? Isaac La Maire, a Dutchman, in 1609. He was not a broker, he was a businessman. He was able to sell more shares than he had. So, he had negative--a broker allowed him to do that. And so, he had a short-interest in VOC, massive short-interest. And people who own VOC shares started thinking, what's going on here? Someone is selling, he's borrowing shares from a broker and selling them. That tends to bring down the price. And there was a downward movement in the Amsterdam stock market. And this guy was blamed for having shorted the stock, and forcing down the price. And so, the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, for two years from 1609 to 1611, banned short-selling, but then they decided to let it go again.

後來有一件醜聞。我讀歷史時看到——那人是誰？Isaac La Maire，荷蘭人，1609 年。他不是經紀人，是商人。他能賣出比他擁有更多的股份，等於負部位——經紀人允許他這樣做。於是他對 VOC 有 massive short-interest。持有 VOC 的人開始覺得：到底怎麼回事？有人在賣，他向經紀人借股來賣，這往往會壓低價格。Amsterdam 股市下跌，大家怪這人放空、壓價。於是 Amsterdam Stock Exchange 在 1609 到 1611 兩年間禁止 short-selling，後來又決定放寬。

The point of all this discussion--I'm telling you a story about Holland 400 years ago, but the reason I'm telling you the story is to try to emphasize how certain things just happen naturally. Once you set the framework up, you set up a big company, and it’s a company that lasts a long time, OK. It's very valuable. Anybody can buy shares in it, OK, so it's democratic. And the value is very uncertain, because this company is going to be in business into the far future. And it's building a whole arrangement, an empire of trading posts and ships, and who knows what it's worth. So, the price is very uncertain, and buying it is a sort of a gamble. And so, the price starts fluctuating wildly. And it attracts all kinds of interest. And some people think it's going to go up, and some people think it's going to go down, and they start debating about this, and wondering about this.

我講這整段——一個 400 年前 Holland 的故事——重點是要強調：某些事情一旦你把框架搭起來，就會自然發生。你成立一家大公司，而且它會存在很久；它很有價值；任何人都能買它的股份，所以很「民主」。但價值很不確定，因為公司要經營到遙遠的未來，要建立貿易站與船隊的整個體系，誰知道值多少？價格很不確定，買它某種程度上像賭博。價格開始劇烈波動，吸引各種關注：有人看多、有人看空，大家辯論、猜測。

And some guy like Isaac La Maire thinks it's going to go down, so he wants to short the stock. He wants to sell, he doesn't want to buy it, he wants to short it, so that he can have a negative quantity. Other people are really positive and excited about it, and they want to buy all they can get. And they want to even buy more than--they want to borrow money to buy the stock. So, you have this tension between the shorts like Isaac La Maire and the gung-ho traders who want to buy it. And it creates a lot of volatility in the market. But the whole effect of this is to create interest in the stock. So, it brings in money. And it ultimately made the VOC very successful, because so many people wanted to give money to this trading company. So, they were able to build hundreds of ships, and set up big outposts all over. And it became very valuable.

像 Isaac La Maire 這種人認為會跌，所以他想 short：他想賣、不想買，用放空讓自己持有「負的數量」。另一些人非常樂觀，想盡量買，甚至借錢買股票。於是 Isaac La Maire 這類 shorts 與積極買進的交易者之間形成張力，市場波動很大。但整體效果是：它創造了對股票的興趣，資金湧入，最終讓 VOC 非常成功，因為太多人想把錢給這家貿易公司。他們得以建造數百艘船、在全球設立大型據點，變得非常有價值。

And it was an invention, kind of a social invention. I'm thinking, it's kind of analogous. We have recent inventions that we think about, the social media. We have, you know, Facebook and other recent inventions. This was an invention like that. It was an invention that got people together and communicating and excited about something. And it created a sort of a game that people were playing that turned out to be productive. That's why it was copied all over the world. So, the core concepts, which began in Holland in 1609, are everywhere now. Every country of the world has this.

這是一種發明，社會性的發明。我覺得它類似我們今天想的 social media：Facebook 等等。它讓人聚在一起、溝通、對某件事興奮，形成一種人們在玩的「遊戲」，最後卻很有生產力。所以它被複製到全世界。這些核心概念從 1609 年代的 Holland 開始，如今各國都有。

I should also add, by the way, that the VOC was a limited liability corporation. Amazing. When I told you that limited liability came in in 1811 in New York. I think I qualified that. It used to be that some companies had in their charter an agreement with the government, that the stockholders had limited liability. What came in in 1811 in New York was a law that said all companies are limited liability. And moreover, anybody in the world can start a--well, anyone in New York can start a company, and it will always be limited liability. So don't worry, you can invest in any company. And you can not worry about being sued for the debts of the company.

我也應補充：VOC 是 limited liability corporation，這很驚人。我上次說 limited liability 在 1811 年進入 New York——我想我有補充過：以前有些公司會在章程裡與政府約定股東有限責任。1811 年 New York 的法律則是：所有公司都是 limited liability；而且任何人——至少在 New York——都能成立公司，且一律 limited liability。所以你不必擔心投資任何公司會因公司債務被追訴到個人。

Well, back then, Holland didn't go that far, but they did create one company that did have limited liability. So what that meant was, you could invest in this company, and it's just a game, you know? I can't lose more than I put into it. And if these guys turn out to be crooks and some of them are hanged for their crimes, no problem with me, because I'm an innocent investor. The law doesn't require that I investigate, you know, whether the guys who run the company are really honest. Let's protect investors. So, all you can lose is the money you put in.

當時 Holland 沒走到那麼遠，但他們確實創造了一家具有 limited liability 的公司。意思是：你可以投資這家公司，它像一場遊戲——你最多只會損失你投入的金額。若經營者是壞人、有人因罪被吊死，也與我無關，因為我是無辜投資人。法律也不要求我去調查經營者是否誠實；我們要保護投資人。所以你最多只會失去你投入的錢。

So, it created a tremendous opportunity. It was talked about, because the stock price went up and up and up, and it made people rich who invested in it. But it was also very volatile. It went up and down. People had never seen anything like this before, because nothing was so actively traded, and had such an interesting story that you could change your mind about from one day to the next. Anyway, I didn't want just tell stories. This is a story, though, that illustrates our last lecture. It was a breakthrough innovation. It was a kind of gambling, but not gambling. It was gambling on real things. And so, you know, people like to gamble, but, you know, it's usually a waste of their time. This is not a waste time. This was setting up trading around the world. And so, it was important, it was a very important innovation.

這創造了巨大機會。大家談論它，因為股價一路上去，讓投資者致富；但它也非常波動，上上下下。人們從未見過如此活躍交易、故事又如此引人、讓你一天到另一天都能改變想法的東西。總之，我不是只想講故事；但這故事能說明上一講：它是突破性創新。它有賭博的成分，又不是純賭博——它是賭在真實事物上。人們喜歡賭，但通常浪費時間；這不是浪費時間，這是在建立全球貿易。所以它很重要，是非常重要的創新。

## Chapter 3. The Equity Premium Puzzle｜第三章：股權溢價謎題（The Equity Premium Puzzle）

But now I want to use it as a lead-in to the main theme of this lecture, which is about portfolio management and risk. And the first concept I wanted to talk about is leverage. Well, and also let me add the equity premium. These are the two main concepts. Maybe I'll do equity premium first. Here's the conundrum that people were presented with. And I'll stay on the VOC story, but it's much more general than that. VOC, after a few years out, people thought, you know, this company is amazing. It's just growing so fast, it's making so much money, it might have a really high return, like unbelievably high, like 20% a year, or even more, but let's say 20% a year. And that's what generated the excitement.

但現在我要用它導入本講主題：投組管理與風險。我想談的第一個概念是槓桿（leverage）。另外還有 equity premium。這是兩大概念。我或許先談 equity premium。人們面對的難題如下。我仍用 VOC 故事，但它遠比 VOC 更一般化。幾年後大家覺得 VOC 太驚人：成長快、賺錢多，報酬可能非常高，難以置信，例如每年 20% 或更高；我們先假設 20%。這就是興奮的來源。

But some people wondered, well, how can it be? Maybe it's earned 20%, but how can it consistently do that? So, let me put ''puzzle.'' We've gone through 400 years of history since the VOC was established. And since then, it seems to be remaining true that companies' shares do extremely well. And that's a puzzle. Because you know, if you can make a high return on some investment, wouldn't you think that enough people would flock into the investment, so that it no longer--you know, too many people trying to do this, so it's no longer performing so well? But in fact, it seems like the average return on stocks has been very high.

但有人疑惑：怎麼可能？也許某段時間賺了 20%，但怎麼能持續？所以我稱之為「謎題（puzzle）」。從 VOC 成立至今已 400 年，似乎仍然成立的一件事是：公司股票表現極好。這是謎題。因為若某投資能賺高報酬，你會以為足夠多人湧入，直到它不再——太多人做同一件事，報酬就不再那麼好。但事實上，股票的平均報酬似乎一直很高。

This is a theme in Jeremy Siegel's book Stocks for the Long Run, which I have on the reading list. Siegel has data, doesn't go back to 1602, but it goes back to the nineteenth century. And he says that the geometric average return, annual return, on the United States stock market from 1871 to 2006 was 6.8% a year, corrected for inflation. That's 6.8% a year after inflation. So, if you're at 3% or 4% inflation, right, that's 10% a year. Let's compare that with short-term governments [correction: government bonds], which are the safest thing in the United States. The average real return on them was only 2.8% a year. So, the difference is 4% a year. So, for well over 100 years in the United States, stocks performed extremely well.

So, what do I want to do? I want to get the highest expected return for any standard deviation. I want to take a line that goes through 5% on the y-axis and is as high as possible. So, I'm taking a point right over here, at 5%, and trying to get as high as I can. It turns out then, that I want to pick the point which has a tangency with the Efficient Portfolio Frontier. And so, that means the highest straight line that touches the Efficient Portfolio Frontier. And so, now I can achieve any point on that line. And that's again Markowitz's insight. 我想要什麼？對任何給定標準差，要最高預期報酬。也就是從縱軸 5%（無風險利率）出發，畫一條盡可能陡、盡可能高的直線。結果你要選與有效前沿相切（tangency）的那點：最高的切線碰到前沿。然後你可以達到切線上任一點——又是 Markowitz 的洞見。若選切點，權重大概像 11% oil、30% stocks、50% government bonds 之類（講者澄清：口頭猜的數字加總可能不剛好 100%，但真正解會加總為 100%）。那代表沒有去 short 無風險利率意義下的「舉債槓桿部位」（講者澄清）。

But I could get even higher returns, if my client wants that, by leveraging. I would borrow and buy even more of this risky portfolio. So, this portfolio here is called the Tangency Portfolio. And what Markowitz's theory shows is that, once you add the risky asset [correction: riskless asset], the relevant Efficient Portfolio Frontier is now really this tangency line. And so, I want to do a mixture of the riskless asset and the Tangency Portfolio that accords with my risk preferences. But I don't want to ever just move to one of these other portfolios. Because, [each of] these other portfolios, like 15% oil, 53% stock, 32% bonds, is dominated, has a higher expected return for the same risk, by a portfolio of the Tangency Portfolio, leveraged up a little bit by borrowing.

若客戶要更高報酬，就借錢買更多這個風險投組（槓桿）。這個切點投組稱為 Tangency Portfolio。Markowitz 理論顯示：一旦加入無風險資產（講者口誤說 risky asset 後更正），相關的有效前沿其實是這條切線。你要依風險偏好，在無風險資產與 Tangency Portfolio 之間配置。你不該停在前沿上其他點，例如 15% oil、53% stock、32% bonds，因為那些點會被「Tangency Portfolio 加上一點借款槓桿」在相同風險下提供更高預期報酬所支配（dominated）。

I don't know if it was clear in Markowitz's paper, but it became clear soon after. There really is, in a sense, an optimal portfolio. It's the Tangency Portfolio. Because everyone wants to invest on this line, and any point on this line is a mixture of the riskless asset and the Tangency Portfolio. And so, everyone wants to invest in the same portfolio. So, there is an optimal portfolio, in a sense. It's in a sense that everybody wants to do the same risky investments. People will differ in their risk preferences, and so some of them will want to do a riskier, a more leveraged version, and some of them will do a less leveraged version of the Tangency Portfolio, but everyone wants to do the Tangency Portfolio. So, that is the key idea of Markowitz's portfolio management. And it's been expressed by some as the Mutual Fund Theorem.

我不確定 Markowitz 原文是否寫得夠清楚，但不久後就很清楚：在某種意義上確實有「最適投組」，就是 Tangency Portfolio。因為大家都想投在這條切線上，而線上每點都是無風險資產與 Tangency Portfolio 的混合。所以大家想做同一個風險投組；差別只在風險偏好：有人槓桿多、有人少，但風險資產那一塊都該是 Tangency Portfolio。這是 Markowitz 投組管理的核心，也被稱為 Mutual Fund Theorem（共同基金定理）。

First of all, I have to just to define for you, what is a mutual fund? You may not know that. A mutual fund is a certain kind of investment company aimed at a retail audience. The idea is that, all we need is one mutual fund. There's thousands of mutual funds to serve investors. Because I had that everyone is investing in the Tangency Portfolio. So, they should call their fund the Tangency Portfolio fund. And our fund is the optimal mix of stock, bonds, oil and whatever else. And then, you don't necessarily want to own only that mutual fund, but you want to own mixtures of that mutual fund and the riskless asset.

先定義 mutual fund：一種面向散戶的投資公司型態（也可叫 investment company theorem，但歷史用語如此）。mutual 指基金股份持有人就是唯一一類所有人，彼此平等——但這對你不太重要。重點是：理論上我們只需要一檔 mutual fund；世上卻有成千上萬檔，因為理論上大家都該持有 Tangency Portfolio。乾脆把基金取名 Tangency Portfolio fund，裡面是股票債券 oil 等的最適混合。你不一定要只持有那檔基金，但你的部位應該是那檔基金與無風險資產的混合。

So, you only need one investment company. See, I told you this story. I said, imagine that you were mathematically inclined, and you have all the statistics, and you're going to figure it out. What's the best thing to do? We've just figured it out. I haven't gone through all the math details. There is a best thing to do. You should offer, as your investment product, the Tangency Portfolio. And that's it. Once you've figured it out, there's nothing more to do. There's no need to hire any more finance people. You've figured it out, according to Markowitz's theory. And all the investors in the world will just invest in this one. And that's [it]. Case closed. We don't need thousands of mutual funds. Under the assumptions of Markowitz, which is that we're agreed on the variances, and covariances, and expected returns, there's a single optimal risky portfolio. And then the instructions to investors are very simple. All you need is two assets in your portfolio. The mutual fund that owns the Tangency Portfolio, and whatever amount of debt you want. So, if you're footloose and fancy free, you can even leverage it. You can borrow and [do] 2-to-1, 3-to-1, it's up to your tastes. But you don't need to look at anything other than the mutual fund.

所以你只需要一家投資公司。還記得我說的：你很數學、有全部統計量，要算出最好做法——我們剛算完了（細節數學此處不展開）。你該提供的產品就是 Tangency Portfolio，然後就結束了；理論上不必再雇更多金融人員。全世界投資人都買這一檔就好，案件關閉，不需要上千檔基金。在 Markowitz 假設下（大家對變異數、共變數、預期報酬有共識），只有一個最適風險投組。投資人指令很簡單：投組裡只需要兩種東西——持有 Tangency Portfolio 的 mutual fund，以及你要多少負債／無風險部位。你若愛冒險，甚至可以 2-to-1、3-to-1 槓桿，看口味。你不必再看別的。

So, that's an important insight. And what it means, then--that leads to something else. Markowitz didn't get this idea. It came out later. Someone was thinking, well, if everyone should be investing in the same portfolio, it doesn't add up unless that portfolio is equal to the total assets out there in the world, right? They all want to invest in the same best portfolio. So, that has to be proportional to the market portfolio. So, the Tangency Portfolio equals the market portfolio.

這是很重要的洞見，並導出另一件事：Markowitz 自己沒走到這步，後來才出現。有人想：若大家都該投同一投組，那除非這投組等於世界上所有可投資資產的總合比例，否則加不起來，對吧？若 oil 總量是 stock 的兩倍，Tangency Portfolio 裡 oil 也必須是 stock 的兩倍，否則供需對不起來。大家最終得持有整個市場。所以 Mutual Fund Theory 意味著 market portfolio 等於 Tangency Portfolio（講者先前說 proportional，後更正為等於）。若所有投資人都依 Markowitz 模型思考，他們都想做同一件事、買同一個「最好風險投組」，那它必須等於市場投組。

It has to add up, so that the market is cleared and all the VOC stock is owned. And more generally, if there are many assets, all the assets have to end up owned by someone. So, the cardinal implication of this theory is that the market portfolio, which is everything that's out there in the world to invest in, has to be proportional to the Tangency Portfolio [correction: has to be equal to the Tangency Portfolio]. 所以我先前問：為什麼不是每個人都全押 VOC？若 VOC 明顯更好，大家不都該全放 VOC？我們現在理解：不會，因為人們在意風報抵換，會同時持有 VOC 與無風險資產，市場才能出清、所有股票被持有。更一般地，很多資產最後都要有人持有。這套理論的核心結論是：market portfolio（世上可投資的一切）必須等於 Tangency Portfolio（講者更正：不是僅 proportional 的含糊說法，在均衡敘述下應一致）。若此成立——我還有幾張投影片——

Now it's called the Capital Asset Pricing Model in finance. So, that's Capital Asset--which is [a] pricing model, which was not invented by Markowitz, but was invented by Sharpe and Lintner somewhat shortly after Markowitz. The Capital Asset Pricing Model--and I'm not going to derive this equation. But it says the expected return on any asset, the i-th asset, equals the risk free rate, plus the β of that asset times the difference between the expected return on the market and the expected return on the riskless asset.

這在金融裡稱為 Capital Asset Pricing Model（CAPM）。它不是 Markowitz 發明，而是 Sharpe 與 Lintner在 Markowitz 之後不久提出。我不會推導方程式，但它說：第 i 個資產的預期報酬等於無風險利率，加上該資產的 β 乘以（市場預期報酬減無風險利率）。

I was just going to try to explain this intuitively, and then I'll be done. I have one more slide about the Sharpe ratio. But the intuitive idea--let me just say, everything should have a very simple explanation. And the intuitive idea is this: Starting from Markowitz, we got an understanding of what risk is. People didn't clearly appreciate that. People used to think that risk was uncertainty, right, in finance. If a stock has a lot of uncertainty, that uncertainty means that it's a dangerous stock, and people will demand a high expected return, otherwise they won't hold the stuff. But the CAPM says no, people don't care about the uncertainty of the stock. Because if it's one stock out of many, they'll put it in their portfolio, and if it's independent of everything else, it all gets averaged out, and so, who cares? So, people don't care about variance.

我想用直覺解釋，然後就結束。還有一張 Sharpe ratio 的片子。直覺是：從 Markowitz 我們理解風險是什麼；以前人們不夠清楚。以前人以為風險就是不確定性：不確定性高就危險，投資人會要求高預期報酬才持有。但 CAPM 說：不，人們不在乎單一股票的不確定性。若它只是很多股票之一，放進投組後若與其他獨立，會被平均掉，誰在乎？所以人們不在乎單純變異數（variance）。

Well, what is it that people care about? People care about covariance. This is the basic insight that followed from Markowitz. People care about how much a stock moves with the market, because that's what costs me something. I don't care, I can own a million little stocks that all have independent risk. It all averages out, doesn't mean anything to me. I'll put them in tiny quantities in my portfolio. But if they correlate with the market, I can't get rid of the risk, because it's the big picture risk. That's what insurance companies, that's what everyone cares about. It's this market risk. The big risk. You only care about how much a stock correlates with the big picture in its risk.

那人在乎什麼？共變數（covariance）。這是 Markowitz 之後的基本洞見：人在乎股票跟市場一起動多少，因為那才會讓你付出代價。我可以持有百萬檔彼此獨立的小股票，風險平均掉，無所謂，我每檔放很小。但若它與市場相關，我就無法消掉那種大格局風險——保險公司與一般投資人在乎的都是 market risk（市場風險）。你只在乎股票風險裡與大市場共動的那部分。

So, that's measured by β. The β is the regression--the slope coefficient, when you regress the return on the i-th asset on the return of the market. So, high β stocks are stocks that go with the market. We found out that Apple has a β of 1.5, or roughly that. That means, they respond in an exaggerated way. It's not 1, it's greater than 1, they more than move with the market. And so, investors will demand a higher return on Apple stock, because its β is greater than for other stocks. That's the core idea that underlies it. Do you see that intuitively? So, you have to change your idea of what risk is. Risk is covariance. It's co-movements.

這用 β 衡量：β 是把第 i 資產報酬對市場報酬迴歸的斜率。高 β 股票更跟市場一起走。例如 Apple 的 β 約 1.5，表示它對市場波動放大反應（大於 1）。因此投資人會要求 Apple 更高報酬，因為 β 較高。你直覺上能理解嗎？所以你要改變「風險」的觀念：風險是共變數，是共同波動（co-movements）。

I have just one more slide here. It's named after William Sharpe, who is the inventor, with Lintner, of the Capital Asset Pricing Model. The Sharpe ratio is, for any portfolio, the average return on the portfolio minus the risk-free rate, divided by the standard deviation of the portfolio. And if you take the CAPM model, the Sharpe ratio is constant along the tangency line.

最後一張片子。Sharpe ratio 以 William Sharpe 命名（他與 Lintner 發明 CAPM）。對任何投組，Sharpe ratio 是（投組平均報酬減無風險利率）除以投組報酬標準差。在 CAPM 架構下，沿著切線 Sharpe ratio 是常數。

This is a way of correcting the average return from some investment for leverage. The idea is--some companies used to advertise, we've had a 15% average return, and then investors would say, but wait a minute. You didn't tell me what your leverage is. That's the first thing you should learn from this course. Someone advertises that they had 15% return, you say, ha, I want to know what your leverage was. I want to know--you were just leveraging it up and taking big risks, and on average you'll do well, but it's risky.

這是把平均報酬用槓桿調整的一種方法。以前有些公司廣告：我們平均報酬 15%；投資人會說：等等，你槓桿多少？這是本課第一個要學的：有人宣稱 15% 報酬，你要問槓桿。你可能只是槓桿拉高、承擔大風險，平均上會好，但很風險。

So, this is the correction you make. How do you correct for leverage? You might say, well I want to look at what fraction of the investment portfolio is in the risky asset, and what fraction is in the riskless asset. And so, you have to go one step further and undo the leverage for that company. It's hard to do that. But the easy thing to do is just calculate the Sharpe ratio for the investment company. So, if some guy is investing and claiming to have done 15% of return per year on his portfolio, well, I'm going to look at the standard deviation of the portfolio.
