# Financial Markets (2011), ECON 252 Lecture 19｜Yale ECON252（2011）第19講：投資銀行與來賓座談（Jon Fougner）｜中英對照稿

## Chapter 1. Key Elements of Investment Banking｜第一章：投資銀行的核心要素

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

All right. Now, are we in order? OK. All right. Today, we want to talk about investment banking, which is different from commercial banking. And today we have a guest, Jon Fougner, who took this course almost 10 years ago and has been working in investment banking since. I'll introduce him in a few minutes, but I wanted to start with just the elements of investment banking, and then I wanted to talk about changes in it that came about after the financial crisis of 2007 through 2009. OK.

好，我們都準備好了嗎？好。今天我們要談投資銀行（investment banking），它和商業銀行（commercial banking）不同。今天我們有一位來賓 Jon Fougner，他大約十年前修過這門課，之後一直在投資銀行業工作。我幾分鐘後會介紹他，但我想先從投資銀行的基本要素談起，接著再談 2007 到 2009 年金融危機之後它出現了哪些變化。

The topic is investment banking. And that is a term, a 20th-century term, that first became big and important, I'd say, in the 1930s, but preceded that by some years. And it refers to a business of helping other businesses create securities. If someone wants to issue stock, they go to an investment banker to help them. Or if you want issue bonds, you go to an investment banker. It can be a corporation that goes to the—for-profit, it can be a non-profit corporation, it can be a government. I suppose even an individual, who is incorporated, can go to an investment bank. That's the investment banking business.

主題是投資銀行。這個詞是二十世紀的用語，我會說大約在 1930 年代變得又大又重要，但更早幾年就已出現。它指的是一種業務：協助其他企業創造證券（securities）。若有人要發行股票，會去找投資銀行家幫忙；若要發行債券，也是找投資銀行家。客戶可以是營利公司、非營利法人、政府；我想即使是已設立法人的個人，也可以找投資銀行。這就是投資銀行業。

Now it differs—it shares something with the consulting business, because investment bankers serve often as consultants. A company will come to an investment banker with a problem, and they want to raise money by issuing new shares, for example, to solve that problem. But if it's a good investment bank, they will do more than just issue shares for them. They'll talk about their whole corporate strategy. So, in that sense, an investment bank looks like a consulting firm, but they don't do pure consulting. That makes the distinction. Maybe, they're in many ways a favored consultant, because they bring money, all right? You can talk to a consultant, who will bring you no money, and another consultant, who has his hands on money somewhere. And that helps a lot. The advice and the money together help a lot.

它和諮詢業有共同之處——投資銀行家常常扮演顧問。公司帶著問題來找投資銀行，例如想透過發行新股籌錢以解決問題。但若是一家好的投資銀行，不會只做發股；還會談整體公司策略。所以在某種意義上，投資銀行像諮詢公司，但不是純諮詢，差別在這裡。他們在很多方面可說是「更受青睞的顧問」，因為他們帶得來資金：你可以找一位不會給你錢的顧問，也可以找一位手邊摸得到資金的顧問，那差很多。建議加上資金，一起來幫助很大。

So, investment bankers are different from traders, because usually they deal with creating something—about making a corporation or a government—making it work, enabling them to do something that they want to do. And then, being realistic about it, and coming up with the money to do it. And so, that's how investment banking differs from trading. And it differs from commercial banking in that a pure investment bank does not accept deposits. You can't go to your investment bank and say, I'd like to open a savings account. They don't do it, OK? I'm talking about a pure investment bank.

投資銀行家與交易員（traders）不同，因為他們通常處理的是「創造」——讓公司或政府能運作、能做想做的事，務實評估，並籌出資金。所以投資銀行與交易不同。它與商業銀行的不同在於：純投資銀行不接受存款。你不能走進投資銀行說「我要開儲蓄帳戶」——他們不做這個。我說的是純投資銀行。

But let me just give you something about this business. I'm going to come in a moment to point out that most investment banking businesses are not pure investment banks. But let's talk about what a pure investment bank does. It does underwriting of securities. That means—suppose you're a company and you want to issue shares. You need someone to go to bat for you, someone who knows the kind of people who might buy your shares, and can vouch for you. So in some sense, it's a reputation thing. The investment bank has contacts among people who make big investments, and they manage the issuance of your new shares, and that's called an underwriting.

先讓我對這行多說一點。等一下我會指出：多數投資銀行業務其實不是「純」投資銀行。但我們先談純投資銀行做什麼。它做證券承銷（underwriting）。意思是——假設你是公司、想發行股票，你需要有人為你背書、認識可能買你股票的人並能為你擔保。所以某種意義上這是聲譽（reputation）的事。投資銀行在大型投資人之間有人脈，並管理你新股的發行流程，這就叫承銷。

If it's the first time you've issued shares, it's called an IPO, or initial public offering. So, you're a private company, it's just you and a few friends own the company, but now you want to go public, you would generally go to an investment bank, and talk to them about how to do it. And the investment bank would solve that problem for you by doing an underwriting. So, traditionally there's two kinds of underwriting. Also, there's also something called a seasoned offering, and that means, for a company that has already gone public, and it already has shares traded, so that the shares are seasoned, but you want to issue more shares. So, you can go to an investment bank to do that, as well. OK.

若你第一次發行股票，就叫 IPO（initial public offering，首次公開發行）。你是私人公司，只有你和少數朋友持股，現在要上市，通常會找投資銀行談怎麼做，投資銀行透過承銷幫你解決。傳統上有兩類承銷方式。還有一種叫 seasoned offering：公司已經上市、股票已在交易（股份已「成熟」），但你想再發更多股，也可以找投資銀行做。

There's two kinds of deals. There's a bought deal, and then there's a best efforts. With a bought deal, the investment bank buys your shares. They go in and say, you know, we know that we can get market for your shares. We will buy them ourselves and resell them on the market. A best efforts offering is one where the investment bank doesn't buy it and doesn't promise anything. They say, we'll make our best efforts to place this offering, OK? So, those are the basic things that they do. The methods that they use are regulated by the Securities and Exchange Commission in order to make—the SEC in the United States, and regulated similarly in other countries. So, that's the basic investment banking business.

交易型態有兩種：bought deal 與 best efforts。bought deal 是投資銀行買下你的股票；他們說我們有把握為你的股票找到市場，我們先自己買下再轉賣到市場。best efforts 則是投資銀行不買下、也不保證任何結果，只說會盡最大努力（best efforts）把這檔發行安置出去。這些是他們做的基本事。做法受美國 SEC（Securities and Exchange Commission）等機關監管，其他國家也有類似規範。以上就是投資銀行業的基本輪廓。

So, if you're thinking of where to place yourself, I think investment banking suits very well people who are—it's not good for autistic people. If you're autistic, be a trader, OK? Then, you just get on the phone, and you buy and sell all day, and you can be rude, and you can have coffee stains on your shirt, and you don't have to know anything about classical music. OK? But investment bankers are a different—I see Jon is laughing. Tell me, what you know about classical music. I assume that was a part of your training at Goldman. He says no. It's a whole different industry. So, if you go to the symphony and look around, you'll see lots of investment bankers there. But you won't see any traders. You nod on that, maybe.

所以若你在想自己適合哪裡，我覺得投資銀行很適合某類人——對自閉症者（autistic）可能不太適合；若是，去當交易員吧。你就整天打電話買賣，可以粗魯一點、襯衫可以有咖啡漬，也不必懂古典音樂。但投資銀行是另一回事——我看到 Jon 在笑。跟我說你懂多少古典音樂。我以為那是你在 Goldman 的訓練之一。他說沒有。這是完全不同的行業。你去交響音樂會環顧，會看到很多投資銀行家，但看不到交易員。你也許對這點點頭。

We talked about moral hazard. I think that an important part of what investment banks do is, solve a moral hazard problem. And that problem is, that companies, who issue shares, don't have a reputation. And so, what do I care, I'll issue shares, right before we're going to go bankrupt, OK? We know inside that we're going to go bankrupt, so hey, let's just see, if we can milk this company, before the public knows it, and issue shares. That's a moral hazard. And the investment bank is in business to prevent that moral hazard. They do the due diligence, they check you out, and then after that, people are more trusting of you. So, I think investment banking is built around trust, it's establishing trust.

我們談過道德風險（moral hazard）。我認為投資銀行很重要的一環，是解決道德風險。問題在於：要發股票的公司可能還沒有聲譽，那內部人可能想：管他的，我們快破產了，趕在公眾知道前榨乾公司、再發股票。這就是道德風險。投資銀行這行就是要降低這種風險：做盡職調查（due diligence）、查清楚你，之後大家才比較敢信你。所以投資銀行建立在信任上，是建立信任。

So, that's how it differs from a lot of—that's why it's important that these people be cultivated and impressive. They tend to be well-spoken. I can ask Jon, whether he agrees on all this, but it's my impression, you can tell when the investment bankers walk in the room. They dress differently, they look differently. I don't know what it is. It's something about reputation, it's what it's built around.

這也是為什麼這些人要養成得體、令人印象深刻——他們通常很會表達。我可以問 Jon 是否同意，但我的印象是：投資銀行一走進房間你就看得出來。穿著不同、氣質不同。我也說不清，總之與聲譽有關，整個行業就築在這上面。

## Chapter 2. Principles and Culture of Investment Banking｜第二章：投資銀行的原則與文化

The investment banking industry—let me just—since I'm talking about the nature of investment banking and since we have a Goldman Sachs representative here. I put on your reading list a book as an optional reading by Charles Ellis called The Partnership, and it's a history of Goldman Sachs. Goldman Sachs was an investment bank until just very recently, and we'll talk about that. They're still in the investment banking business, but now they're officially a commercial bank. It's an old, venerable firm, and Goldman Sachs emerged in the early 21st century as, I think, the most highly respected and esteemed investment bank in the world. Amazingly successful, and amazingly well-respected.

既然在談投資銀行的本質，而我們現場又有一位 Goldman Sachs 代表——我在閱讀清單上放了一本選讀：Charles Ellis 的 *The Partnership*，是 Goldman Sachs 的歷史。Goldman 直到最近都還是投資銀行，我們等等會談；他們仍做投資銀行業務，但現在在官方分類上是商業銀行。這是一家悠久、受敬重的公司，我認為在二十一世紀初成為全球最受敬重、聲譽最高的投資銀行，極成功也極受尊敬。

Ellis wrote a book just a few years—Ellis is on the Yale Corporation. He's a distinguished businessmen and author himself, and he wrote a book about Goldman Sachs, which is largely admiring. Like, how did this happen? How did this phenomenon of Goldman Sachs come about? And I suggested—I didn't assign—I suggested, you read one chapter, was called Principles. And it says something about Goldman Sachs, and it refers to, in that chapter, the chairman of Goldman Sachs, John Whitehead, in the 1970s wrote down a list of principles that guide Goldman Sachs. And Ellis seems admiring of these principles. Not everyone would agree. It's a matter of taste, I guess, if anything.

Ellis 幾年前寫了這本書——他是 Yale Corporation 成員，本身是傑出商人与作者。書寫 Goldman，大致是讚賞口吻：怎麼會這樣？Goldman 這現象如何形成？我建議——沒有指定——建議讀一章叫 *Principles*，談 Goldman，其中提到 1970 年代 Goldman 董事長 John Whitehead 寫下一系列指引公司的原則。Ellis 對這些原則似乎很欣賞；不是每個人都同意，若有的話，算是品味問題。

Whitehead is now—I just looked it up—he's 88 years old, and is retired from Goldman, must have retired some years ago. What kind of an organization? Ellis says, that the thing that struck him about the organization is loyalty. But that's not alone, that people feel a strong loyalty toward their company. That's not on Whitehead's list.

Whitehead 我剛查了一下——現在 88 歲，已從 Goldman 退休多年。什麼樣的組織？Ellis 說最讓他印象深刻的是忠誠（loyalty）——人們對公司有強烈歸屬感。但這不在 Whitehead 的清單上。

So, Whitehead's list. What is his first principle of Goldman Sachs? "Our client's interests always come first." These sound a little bit like bromides. I'm sorry, but I read them thinking, it is the most successful investment bank in the world, so maybe there's something beyond—I think, there is something beyond platitudes here. Second, "our assets are people, capital, and reputation." That's a coincident with what I said. "Uncompromising determination to achieve excellence." Well, everybody says that, so maybe discount that. "We stress creativity and imagination." Well, those are sort of bromides, maybe.

Whitehead 的清單。第一條是什麼？「客戶利益永遠第一。」聽起來像老生常談；但讀的時候我會想：這是全世界最成功的投資銀行，也許不只有口號——我認為確實不只有口號。第二：「我們的資產是人、資本與聲譽。」這和我前面說的一致。「毫不妥協追求卓越」——大家都這樣講，也許打點折扣。「我們強調創意與想像」——也許也算老生常談。

Then, Whitehead issued some guidelines—this is also in that chapter later—for Goldman Sachs employees, and these seem to be a little bit more candid. "The boss usually decides, not the assistant treasurer. Do you know the boss?" That's sort of something I've learned from my own interaction with people—the boss really does decide, and Goldman Sachs goes for the top. And maybe this is obnoxious, I don't know—they don't want to talk with underlings. "You never learn anything when you're talking." That means, be a good listener. "The respect of one person is worth more than the acquaintance with 100." "There's nothing worse than an unhappy client." The one thing that—I don't if it's on Whitehead's list—but I think it really says something about investment banking, and that Ellis says, is that they shun publicity. They don't want to be in the newspaper, they want to be known by the president. They want to be known by a few prominent people. They're kind of social climbers, in a way. But it's all built around some basic principles of service, and they want to be talking to the top guy, and they don't want to be in the newspaper.

接著 Whitehead 對員工發了一些指引——同一章後面——聽起來比較直白。「通常是老闆決定，不是助理司庫。你認識老闆嗎？」這跟我跟人互動學到的一樣：真的是老闆決定，Goldman 要攻頂層。也許這很討人厭——他們不想跟底下的人談。「說話的時候學不到東西」——意思是當好的聆聽者。「一個人的尊重勝過一百個人的點頭之交。」「沒有比不滿意的客戶更糟的事。」還有一點——我不確定在不在 Whitehead 清單上——但我認為很能說明投資銀行，Ellis 也寫到：他們迴避曝光。不想上新聞，想讓總統級人物認識自己、少數要人認識自己。某種程度上像往上爬的社交者。但底層仍是服務的基本原則：要跟最上層談，不要上新聞。

I'm going to quote Ellis on this. Now I'm quoting Charlie Ellis. I call him Charlie. I know him. He's a friend of mine. "Making money, always and no exceptions, was a principle of Goldman Sachs. Nothing was ever done for prestige. In fact, the most prestigious clients were often charged the most. Absolute loyalty to the firm and to the partnership was expected. Personal anonymity was almost a core value. The real culture of Goldman Sachs was a unique blend of drive for making money and the characteristics of family, in ways that the Chinese, Arabs, and old Europeans would well understand."

我要引用 Ellis 的話。以下引用 Charlie Ellis——我叫他 Charlie，我認識他，是朋友。「賺錢、永遠、沒有例外，曾是 Goldman Sachs 的一項原則。沒有任何事是為了虛名。事實上，最有聲望的客戶往往被收最貴。對公司與合伙關係的絕對忠誠被視為理所當然。個人匿名幾乎是核心價值。Goldman Sachs 真正的文化，是強烈賺錢動機與『家族』特質的獨特結合，中國人、阿拉伯人與老歐洲人會很能理解那種家族感。」

So, I'm giving you a flavor of what an investment bank is. You might be repelled by it. You know, is making money so important? And if you are repelled by it, you probably don't want to work for Goldman Sachs. On the other hand, they're kind of respecting some economic principles, right? Working for a firm like this, you can make huge amounts of money, and then at the end, you can give it all away to charity. And that's the new capitalism, right? So, what's wrong with that? What are you going to do with all this? If you make $100 million, what are you going to do with it? You're going to give it away, right? I mentioned at the beginning, I mentioned Andrew Carnegie's book, The Gospel of Wealth. Maybe that's what this is all about. On the other hand, some of them don't give it away, and some of them live lavishly. Different people have different impressions of this business. But I want to make sure I have time for our guest and I'm sort of running out of time.

我在讓你感受投資銀行是什麼。你可能覺得反感——賺錢真的那麼重要嗎？若你反感，大概不會想進 Goldman。另一方面，他們也算尊重某些經濟邏輯：在這種公司工作可以賺很多，最後再全部捐給慈善，這不就是新資本主義嗎？有什麼不對？若你賺一億美元你要怎麼用？捐出去，對吧？我一開始提過 Andrew Carnegie 的《The Gospel of Wealth》，也許重點就在這。但也有人捐得少、生活奢華。大家對這行的印象不同。我得留時間給來賓，時間有點緊了。

## Chapter 3. Regulation of Investment Banking｜第三章：投資銀行的監管

I wanted to talk about what has happened in the crisis. There's so much to say about this topic. Maybe, I should talk first about the first crisis. In 1933, the U.S. Congress passed the Glass-Steagall Act, which forced investment banks—it prevented investment banks from doing commercial banking, or commercial banks from doing investment banking. It split them in two, and it said you have to decide, are you a commercial bank, or are you an investment bank? The Glass-Steagall Act was the act that created the FDIC, the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, the first successful national deposit insurance act in the world. And part of it—it makes sense—if you're going to insure the commercial banks, you better watch what they're doing and prevent them from doing dangerous business. So, the dangerous business was investment banking, and they forced companies to decide.

我想談危機之後發生了什麼。這主題能講的很多。也許先談第一次「危機」：1933 年美國國會通過 Glass-Steagall Act，禁止投資銀行做商業銀行業務、或商業銀行做投資銀行業務，把兩者拆開，要你選邊：你是商業銀行還是投資銀行？Glass-Steagall 也創立了 FDIC（Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation），是世界上第一個成功的全國性存款保險制度。其中一部分——說得通——若你要為商業銀行保險，就得盯緊他們在做什麼、阻止危險業務；當時認定的危險業務就是投資銀行業務，因此強迫公司分流。

So, J.P. Morgan, which was doing both investment banking and commercial banking in 1933 had to decide. What is it? Investment banking or commercial banking? So, they picked commercial banking, and that means they fired all their investment bankers. So, these guys regrouped and they formed an investment bank, called Morgan Stanley. Harold Stanley was a Yale graduate and Henry S. Morgan was, I think—not J.P. Morgan, it was his grandson. Morgan died around 1913. And so, those were two separate one. J.P. Morgan, commercial bank. Morgan Stanley, investment bank.

所以 1933 年同時做兩種業務的 J.P. Morgan 必須決定：到底選哪一邊？他們選了商業銀行，意思是裁掉所有投資銀行部門的人。那些人重新集結，成立了投資銀行 Morgan Stanley。Harold Stanley 是 Yale 畢業，Henry S. Morgan 我想是——不是 J.P. Morgan 本人，是孫子輩。Morgan 本人約在 1913 年過世。於是分成兩家：J.P. Morgan 商業銀行、Morgan Stanley 投資銀行。

But since then, we've repealed the Glass-Steagall Act, and that occurred with the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of—what was that—1999. ## Chapter 4. Shadow Banking and the Repo Market｜第四章：影子銀行與回購市場（Repo）

Well, there's a reading I have on your reading list by Professor Gary Gorton here at Yale, who argues that Lehman, like many other investment banks, was financing a lot of proprietary investments by issuing repos, or by dealing in repos. What is a repo? That's short for repurchase agreement. The banking crisis, that we saw in 2008, was substantially a run on the repo.

舉例：Lehman Brothers，純投資銀行，現在破產、不見了。因為是純投資銀行，不像商業銀行那樣受監管。當時在 Volcker Rule、Dodd-Frank 之前，2008 年破產，是危機最糟的時刻之一。為何破產？你們閱讀清單上有 Yale 的 Gary Gorton 教授文章，他認為 Lehman 與許多投資銀行一樣，大量用 repo（發行或操作 repurchase agreement）為自營投資融資。什麼是 repo？就是 repurchase agreement 的縮寫。2008 年我們看到的銀行業危機，很大一部分是對 repo 的擠兌（run on the repo）。

So, here's what happened, according to Gorton and others who agree with him. Investment banks, like Lehman Brothers, were not regulated like commercial banks, and as long as they didn't accept deposits, they didn't have to be regulated as commercial banks. 依 Gorton 與同意他的人所說：像 Lehman 這類投資銀行不像商業銀行那樣受監管；只要不接受存款，就不必像商業銀行那樣被監管。所以他們可做很多事，又被視為承銷商，好像都可以。不盡然如此，但確實不像商業銀行那麼重監管。Lehman 開始透過 repo 市場實質舉債，大量投資次貸證券與其他證券。
