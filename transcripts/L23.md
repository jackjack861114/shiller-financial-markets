# Financial Markets (2011), ECON 252 Lecture 23｜Yale ECON252（2011）第23講：在金融資本主義世界中找尋你的使命｜中英對照稿

## Chapter 1. The Course and Its Major Themes in Retrospect｜第一章：課程與主要主題的回顧

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

All right. This is the concluding lecture for *Financial Markets.* And in this lecture, I want—oh, I titled this lecture *Finding Your Purpose in a World of Financial Capitalism*—but I just want to give a lot of summary thoughts about the course and about your place in the world of business and finance.

好，這是「Financial Markets」的結語講座。在這一講裡，我本來——喔，我把這講題取名為「在金融資本主義的世界裡，找到你的目的（Finding Your Purpose in a World of Financial Capitalism）」——但我真正想做的，是給大家很多關於這門課、以及你們在商業與金融世界裡位置的總結性想法。

So, we had two—well, we had the major textbook for this course was by Fabozzi et al., and it gave you a lot of detailed information about financial markets and institutions. Did you like it? I'm getting approval, I guess. I put you through something, because I thought you have to know that material. Finance is like a language. Well, it is a language. There's a lot of jargon, and behind the jargon are concepts, and I wanted you to immerse yourself in that.

我們有兩——嗯，這門課的主要教科書是 Fabozzi 等人寫的，它提供大量關於金融市場與金融機構的細節。你們喜歡嗎？我猜我得到的是肯定的反應。我讓你們吃不少苦，因為我認為你們必須知道那些材料。金融像一種語言。嗯，它就是一種語言。有很多行話，而行話背後是概念；我希望你們把自己浸在裡面。

I also assigned my manuscript for my new book, which is tentatively entitled *Finance and the Good Society.* I'm still not sure that that will be the final title. Some of you have been offering me suggested titles, I appreciate that. You can never know what the title of a book will be before you publish it, because someone else can always grab the title, and then you've got to change it.

我也指定了我新書的手稿，暫定書名是 *Finance and the Good Society*。我仍不確定那會不會是最後的書名。有些同學一直給我建議書名，我很感謝。在出版前你永遠無法確定書名會是什麼，因為別人永遠可能先搶走那個書名，然後你就得改。

But that book was about—see, Fabozzi is more about all of the language of finance, and all of the technical details. I wanted to supplement it with something about the purpose of all this, and how it fits into our lives. So, it's not done yet, as you may be well aware in reading it. My apologies. But I benefit from interacting with you about it. It's a dynamic thing.

但那本書想談的是——你看，Fabozzi 更偏向金融的整套語言與所有技術細節。我想用另一本東西補上「這一切的目的」，以及它如何進入我們的生活。所以它還沒寫完，你們讀的時候大概也很清楚。抱歉。但跟你們互動討論它，對我很有幫助。它是動態的。

I have a number of themes. They're all just kind of random thoughts about the major things in this course. But I mean, I'm really thinking this time about the kind of tools we've learned about, tools that are particularly useful for people who specialize in finance. But I think this almost should be a required course for everyone. Maybe I'm just too enthusiastic about it, but the way things get done in our society is through financial arrangements. And too many people talk in vague terms, not *how are we going to make something happen?* And finance is about that, so that's why I think this course should be—there should be more students taking it than are.

我有一堆主題。它們都像是對這門課大事的隨機想法。但我這次真正在想的是：我們學到的那些工具，特別是對專攻金融的人很有用的工具。不過我覺得這門課幾乎應該變成所有人的必修。也許只是我過度熱情，但在我們社會裡，事情之所以會被完成，往往是透過金融安排。太多人用很空泛的方式講話，而不是「我們要怎麼讓某件事真的發生？」金融就是在談這個；所以我才覺得這門課應該——應該要有更多學生來修。

I also said that I think that finance is not a purpose in itself, it's a tool. And that you should be building your life around some kind of purpose. There's a million different purposes, so that's something for you to create in your own mind. But that's where the meaning of life comes from.

我也說過，我認為金融本身不是目的，它是工具。你應該把人生建立在某種目的之上。目的有千千萬萬種，所以那要靠你們自己在心裡創造。但人生意義的來源就在那裡。

So, another thing I've emphasized in this course is that finance is like engineering, so you have to design it. And once something is designed and it works, it gets copied all over the world. You all have learned how to drive a car. Is there anyone here who hasn't driven a car? I won't ask for—no one raised their hand. So, you have to know a little bit about mechanics to drive a car. Maybe not too much. But ultimately, what I wanted to do in this course is, maybe not teach you how to build a car, but how to drive a truck, something big and powerful, and get you beyond the simple things.

所以，這門課我還強調的另一件事是：金融像工程，你得設計它。而一旦某個設計被做出來而且有效，它就會被複製到全世界。你們都學過開車。這裡有人沒開過車嗎？我不會要——沒人舉手。所以開車你得懂一點機械。也許不用太多。但歸根究柢，我這門課想做的，也許不是教你怎麼造車，而是教你怎麼開卡車——某種又大又有力量的東西——並讓你超越那些太簡單的層次。

So, finance, what does it do? It does really important things. It helps allocate scarce resources, it incentivizes people to do good work, and it manages risks. And this is what makes for the developed world that we have now.

那麼，金融做什麼？它做非常重要的事。它幫忙配置稀缺資源、激勵人們好好工作，並管理風險。這也就是我們如今所擁有的「已開發世界」之所以成形的部分原因。

Another theme of this course is about information technology, which is something that's rapidly expanding. I don't have to tell you that. But I think that it will change the world of finance. The last 50 years have shown tremendous changes; the next 50 years will show even more dramatic changes.

這門課的另一個主題是資訊科技，它正在快速擴張。這不用我多說。但我認為它會改變金融世界。過去 50 年已經出現巨大變化；未來 50 年會更戲劇性。

Or anyway, more specifically, I have seven themes that I want to cover in today's lecture.

總之，更具體地說，我今天這講想談七個主題。

The first one is just about the morality of finance. I've been talking about this here and again, but let me say a little bit more about that in concluding. My second theme is hopelessness. There's a tendency for people to think that, at some level, because of the world's problems, it's all hopeless anyway. I don't think it is. Well, I've come to—I'll have to tell you what I mean by that. Then thirdly, I just want to say something about financial theory. Then fourth, to come back to another theme, which is wealth and poverty, which I've talked about a lot. Then, the world of the next century. And I think one trend we'll see is the democratization of finance, that finance will become much more of an integral part of our lives in a new information-technology-enriched world. And then lastly, I'll say something about your career, whether it's in finance or in something completely different. But I'm thinking that a good chance it has something to do with finance.

第一個是金融的道德性。我這門課斷斷續續談過，但讓我在結語時再多說一點。第二個主題是「絕望」。人們很容易在某個層次上覺得，因為世界問題太多，反正一切都沒希望。我不這麼認為。嗯，我會——我得告訴你我指的是什麼。第三，我想談一點金融理論。第四，回到另一個我談很多的主題：財富與貧窮。然後是下一個世紀的世界。我認為我們會看到的一個趨勢是金融的民主化：在一個被資訊科技豐富了的新世界裡，金融會更深度地進入我們的生活。最後，我會談談你們的職涯，不管在金融業或完全不同領域。但我想多半還是會跟金融有點關係。

## Chapter 2. The Morality of Finance｜第二章：金融的道德性

Let me start, though, with the first thing, which is about morality. I have—actually, the two optional readings I have on this part of the reading list are both about morality. And one is the book by Unger called *Living High and Letting Die.* I think it's a dramatically well-written book, but on the first page of the book, he refers you to UNICEF, which—actually, his book was written before the web got popular. The book is 1994, I think. 1996. He could have referred to the web, but he gave the address. UNICEF is the United Nations—what does the I stand for? Children Educational Fund, but what's the I? Can someone tell me? [Correction: United Nations International Children's Emergency Fund.] They don't actually emphasize what it's spelled out. It's the United Nations fund for children of the world, and that's their website.

不過讓我先從第一件事開始：道德。我在這部分閱讀清單上有兩本指定選讀，其實都關於道德。其中一本是 Unger 的 *Living High and Letting Die*。我覺得它寫得極好、極有戲劇性，但在第一頁他把你指到 UNICEF——其實那本書寫在網路還不普及之前。書是 1994，我想。1996。他本可以指到網路，但他給的是地址。UNICEF 是 United Nations——I 代表什麼？Children Educational Fund？但 I 是什麼？有人知道嗎？[更正：United Nations International Children's Emergency Fund。] 他們其實不太強調全名怎麼拼。那是為世界兒童設立的 United Nations 基金，那就是他們的網站。

So, he opens the book by saying, why don't you get out your checkbook right now and mail in $100 to unicef.org, because the estimate, as of 1996, is that UNICEF can save a child's life for $3. So, you will save the lives of 33 children for your $100 check.

所以他在書一開頭就說：你何不現在拿出支票簿，立刻寄 100 美元到 unicef.org，因為截至 1996 年的估算是，UNICEF 花 3 美元就能救一個孩子的命。所以你那張 100 美元支票能救 33 個孩子。

How can they do that? How can they save a life? I think maybe he's referring to things like vaccination programs, things that are really cheap, that some children are not getting. And so, statistically, you can save a child's life for $3. So, he says, write out a check, but of course I can tell you what to do, and that is, just log on to that and get out your credit card. And I don't know if you can save 33 lives with $100, but maybe you can.

他們怎麼做得到？怎麼救一命？我想他可能指的是疫苗計畫之類非常便宜、但有些孩子卻得不到的東西。所以在統計上，你可以用 3 美元救一個孩子。所以他說：開支票吧；當然我也可以告訴你怎麼做，就是上網、拿出信用卡。我不知道 100 美元能不能救 33 條命，但也許可以。

So, when I first read the book, I thought about that, and then I turned the page—because most of us do—and I realized later, a month later, that I never had written out a $100 check. So, I finally did. I went on to unicef.org and I gave exactly $100, as he called for. Then I started—that was thought-provoking to think about that, because why did I stop at $100? Why is it that most of us don't do that? There are intellectual defenses we have—we think of ourselves as good people—but if you were to see a dying child, you would emotionally be driven to do something, if there was something you could do, right? But somehow, when they're not visible to us, we don't take action to make them visible. What Unger's book consists of is an analysis of all the excuses we give for not doing it, for not doing that sort of thing. So, living our comfortable lives and letting other people die.

所以我第一次讀那本書時，我想了想，然後翻頁——因為我們多數人都這樣——一個月後我才意識到，我從來沒開過那張 100 美元支票。所以我最後還是做了。我上 unicef.org，照他說的捐了整整 100 美元。然後我開始——那很發人深省，因為為什麼我停在 100 美元？為什麼我們多數人不那樣做？我們有一套智識上的自我防衛——我們自認是好人——但如果你看見一個垂死的孩子，只要你能做點什麼，你會在情感上被驅動去做，對吧？但不知為何，當他們對我們不可見時，我們就不採取行動讓他們變得可見。Unger 那本書做的，就是分析我們為什麼不做、為什麼不做那一類事的所有藉口。也就是：過我們舒服的日子，讓別人去死。

And I think it's really an interesting book, because it is referring to a paradox of human behavior. I think of this as at the juncture of philosophy and psychology. And now those two departments are starting to—I understand, I'm not in either one of them—are starting to come together, because they're realizing that philosophical issues are related to psychological issues. So somehow the human spirit is very empathetic and sympathetic in certain dimensions, but not so in others.

我覺得那是本很有趣的書，因為它指向人類行為的一個悖論。我把這想成哲學與心理的交界。現在這兩個系開始——我了解，我不屬於任何一邊——開始靠攏，因為他們意識到哲學問題跟心理問題相關。所以在某種程度上，人類精神在某些面向非常能同理、非常同情，但在其他面向卻不是。

When you read his book, you get a sense of meaninglessness or loss of purpose. It's not entirely comfortable to read it. He has a lot of examples of moral dilemmas. I don't mean to find fault with his book, but when I think further about it, it seems that maybe the book is a bit circumscribed by the kind of moral dilemmas that he poses. In some sense moral dilemmas—it's almost like there's a moral imperative for us to take action to do things. That's sort of what he's getting at. But there's almost a moral imperative to be entrepreneurial to do things.

讀他的書時，你會感到某種無意義或失去目的。讀起來不那麼舒服。他有很多道德兩難的例子。我不是要挑他書的毛病，但再往深想，我覺得那本書可能被他所設定的道德兩難類型限制住了一點。在某種意義上，道德兩難——幾乎變成我們有道德命令要採取行動去做事。那有點像是他想說的。但也有另一種道德命令：要用創業精神去做事。

I mentioned before Paul Allen, who was one of the top people in Microsoft, who made so much money and squandered some of it, apparently, on conspicuous consumption, but on the other hand gives a billion dollars to charity. So it seems to me that—let's not conclude that people who don't write the $100 check are evil, and let's think of the many, many dimensionalities of morality.

我以前提過 Paul Allen，他是 Microsoft 高層之一，賺了很多錢，顯然也把一部分花在炫耀性消費上，但另一方面他捐了十億美元做慈善。所以在我看來——不要下結論說沒開那張 100 美元支票的人就是邪惡；我們要想道德有很多很多維度。

The other book I had on this part of the course was by William Graham Sumner, and it was written over 100 years earlier—1883—called *What the Social Classes Owe Each Other.* And Sumner was actually Yale's first real economist. Interesting person. He graduated Yale College in 1863, and he was hired by Yale as a tutor in mathematics in 1866, and became interested in economics and sociology. He has the distinction of being the first American professor to teach a course called Sociology, and in those days there wasn't the distinction between the social sciences that there is now. So he could teach both sociology and economics.

這部分課程的另一本書是 William Graham Sumner 寫的，早了一百多年——1883——書名 *What the Social Classes Owe Each Other.* Sumner 其實是 Yale 第一位真正意義上的經濟學家。很有意思的人。他 1863 年從 Yale College 畢業，1866 年被 Yale 聘為數學助教，後來對經濟學與社會學產生興趣。他有個榮譽：他是第一位開課名就叫 Sociology 的美國教授；當時社會科學之間還不像現在分得那麼清楚，所以他可以同時教社會學與經濟學。

So in his book, I think he started a Yale tradition of conservative economics that lasted until the 1940s. And then Yale kind of drifted more toward the liberal end. But he writes, anyway, in 1883, "Is it wicked to be rich? Is it mean to be a capitalist?" And he says, first of all, it seems, if capitalists are just richer than other people—he asks, where's the dividing line when someone is rich? So is it wicked to be above that line? But where do you draw the line? And if you learn from Unger, maybe we're all wicked, because anyone who doesn't write a $100 check every day to UNICEF, when children are dying around the world, is wicked.

所以在他的書裡，我想他開啟了 Yale 保守派經濟學的傳統，一路延續到 1940 年代。之後 Yale 比較往自由派那端漂移。但他在 1883 年寫道：「富有是邪惡嗎？當資本家是卑鄙嗎？」他說，首先，如果資本家只是比別人有錢——他問：什麼時候算有錢？分界線在哪？所以在那條線以上是邪惡嗎？但你要把線畫在哪？若你從 Unger 那套學來，也許我們全都邪惡，因為當世界上有孩子正在死去，任何人若沒每天開一張 100 美元支票給 UNICEF，就是邪惡。

What Sumner is saying, and it seems to be a theme that survives the centuries, in favor of capitalists. I'll quote Sumner. "The great gains of a great capitalist in a modern state must be put under the head of wages of superintendents. Anyone who believes that any great enterprise of an industrial character can be started without labor must have little experience of life. Let anyone try to get a railroad built or to start a factory and win reputation for its products, and he will find what obstacles must be overcome, what risks must be taken, what perseverance and courage are necessary."

Sumner 想說的，似乎是幾個世紀下來仍站得住、替資本家辯護的主題。我引用 Sumner：「現代國家裡，偉大資本家的巨大收益，必須歸在監工／管理者的工資這一類底下。若有人相信任何具工業性格的大型事業可以不靠勞動就啟動，那他對人生經驗必定很少。讓任何人試著去蓋一條鐵路、或開一間工廠並讓產品贏得名聲，他就會發現必須克服多少障礙、承擔多少風險，以及需要多少毅力與勇氣。」

I don't know that I entirely agree with Sumner, but he has a point, that part of our morality is to do good for the world by doing things like set up railroads, or Microsoft. And the kind of activities that that entails will create opportunities for conspicuous consumption, but not necessarily make that the defining characteristic of someone who does it. Nobody is perfect, and it's hard to judge people ultimately, but it seems to me that there's almost a moral imperative to entrepreneurship.

我不確定我完全同意 Sumner，但他有個重點：我們道德的一部分，是透過像蓋鐵路或打造 Microsoft 這樣的事來對世界做好事。而這類活動會創造炫耀性消費的機會，但不一定把那變成做事者的定義性特徵。沒人是完美的，最終也很難審判人；但在我看來，幾乎有一種「創業」的道德命令。

## Chapter 3. Hopelessness: Challenging Malthus's Dismal Law｜第三章：絕望——挑戰 Malthus 的黯淡法則

Let me go on to the second theme that I said I would talk about, and that is hopelessness. A lot of people, from their education, get the idea that ultimately there's nothing we can really do. This is one of Unger's—Unger talks about rationalizations we give for not being moral, and he calls it futility is a rationalization. Ultimately there's always going to be starving people in the world, and I can try to help this child, but something's going to get him later, and so there's no point. If you have that kind of sense of futility, it can justify any amount of hedonism.

讓我談第二個我說過要談的主題：絕望。很多人從教育裡得到一種想法：歸根究柢我們其實什麼也做不了。這是 Unger 的——Unger 談我們為了不道德而給的合理化，他把其中一種叫做「把無意義（futility）當合理化」。世上永遠會有挨餓的人；我可以試著幫這個孩子，但之後總有什麼會找上他，所以沒意義。若你有那種徒勞感，它可以為任何程度的享樂主義辯護。

But I think that the classic article that leads most people to that sense of futility, and I assume you know about this already, but it's Malthus, 1798, his essay on—well, the title of it is *Essay on the Principle of Population.* So Thomas Malthus wrote about the population problem. It was such a celebrated essay that he went through six editions of it, but I'm going to quote from the first edition, just to remind you. He said, in 1798, "Population, when unchecked, increased in a geometrical ratio and subsistence for man in an arithmetical ratio." So the population growth follows an exponential growth curve. He says geometric, but it goes like that. Whereas he said, at best, the increase of our ability to produce is linear. He calls that arithmetical. And so the population will run off with all of our resources. There's nothing we can do; population will continue to put pressure on our resources. He says—and I'm quoting again from his 1798 essay—"Population, when unchecked, goes on doubling itself every 25 years. If you go through two 25 periods where there was no check on population growth, it is impossible"—I'm quoting him—"to suppose that the produce could be quadrupled. 我在想另一位重要思想者 Robert K. Merton 的著作，他是 Columbia 的社會學家。他是幫忙發展選擇權理論的經濟學家 Robert Merton 的父親。但 Robert K. Merton 提到他所謂的「世界公民階級（cosmopolitan class）」。他在看社會階級。他挑了 United States 一個小鎮，訪談很多人，想了解他們的階級結構。你認同誰？你對誰忠誠？

He was a deep thinker, I think, and looked at what really seemed to separate people. And he decided that in this little town there were really two classes of people. 我認為他是深刻的思想家，去看真正把人分開的是什麼。他判定在這小鎮裡其實有兩種人。他稱之為 cosmopolitans 與 locals。所以 cosmopolitans 的世界觀非常不同。他們往往不在乎鎮上發生什麼。他們談國家或國際的事。他會聽他們說什麼。他們焦點在外；他們覺得鎮無關緊要，而且職位往往較高。

The locals were people, though, who would talk all about their town, and they would talk about people they know. They seemed to value their connections within the town. And the cosmopolitans would act totally indifferent, and they don't know anybody. They don't know who's the head of the fire department, or who holds the—maybe they know the principal of the school, because they may have their kid in the school, but beyond that they don't know anything about their town.

所以 Merton 那是五十多年前寫的。我感覺 cosmopolitans 與 locals 的分裂還在加深。而且是在全球規模上加深。現在有一個世界級的 cosmopolitan 階級，隨著通訊增加，我們有點往那樣分裂。所以世上把 English 學得很好的人——那是世界語言——以及周遊世界的人、以及懂 finance 的人，正在形成一個社會階級。我認為會有敵意與衝突，但有點更難處理，因為 cosmopolitans 很分散，而且往往是我們的親戚，所以社會對比沒那麼強烈。

But you know, I think that the animosities that we are feeling now have to do with the fact that cosmopolitans know and understand finance, and they have lawyers and advisors. The rest of the population feels excluded from that.

但你知道，我認為我們現在感受到的敵意，跟 cosmopolitans 了解並理解 finance、他們有律師與顧問有關。其餘人口覺得被排除在外。

## Chapter 7. The Democratization of Finance｜第七章：金融的民主化

So cosmopolitans know how to get things done, how to raise capital, and they know how to manage their risks, so they don't get into trouble.
