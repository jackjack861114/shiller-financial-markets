## Chapter 1. Introduction｜第一章：導論

**Professor Robert Shiller｜Robert Shiller 教授：**

We're talking about the theory of debt and interest rates. So, I want to talk about a number of technical topics first. We're going to start with a model, an Irving Fisher model of interest. And then I'm going to talk about present values, and discount bonds, compound interest, conventional bonds, the term structure of interest rates, and forward rates. These are all technical things. There are two assignments for this lecture. One is several chapters out of the Fabozzi manuscript. The book is not done, so I think the real reference for this is the Fabozzi et al. manuscript, at this point. And then, Oliver will give a TA section that will clarify, I think, some of the points.

## Chapter 2. Theories for the Determinants of Interest Rates｜第二章：利率決定因素之理論

So anyway, what we're talking about today is interest rates. The percent that you earn on a loan, or that you pay on a loan, depending on what side of it you are. And interest rates go back thousands of years. It's an old idea. Typically, it's a few percent a year, right? The first question we want to try to think about is what explains that. Why is it a few percent a year? And why not something completely different? And why is it even a positive number? Ever think of negative interest rates? Well, these are basic questions.

總之，我們今天要談的是利率：你在貸款上賺到的、或你為貸款支付的百分比，端看你站在哪一邊。利率可以回溯數千年，這是很古老的概念。通常一年是幾個百分點，對吧？我們想先問：這要如何解釋？為什麼一年大約是幾個百分點？為什麼不是完全不同的數字？為什麼它甚至是正數？你想過負利率嗎？這些都是很基本的問題。

So, I wanted to start with the history of thought and an economist from the 19th century, Eugen von Boehm-Bawerk, who wrote a book on the theory of interest in the late nineteen century. Actually it was 1884. And it’s a long, very verbose account of what causes interest rates. But basically, he came up with three explanations. Why is the interest rate something like 5%, or 3%, or 7%, or something in that range? And he said, there are really three causes. One of them is technical progress. That, as the economy gets more and more scientific information about how to do things, things get more productive. So, maybe the 3% percent, or the 5%, whatever it is, is the rate of technical progress. That's how fast technology is improving.

我想從思想史開始：十九世紀經濟學家 Eugen von Boehm-Bawerk 在十九世紀末（其實是 1884 年）寫了一本關於利息理論的書。書很長、非常囉嗦地說明利率的成因。但基本上他提出三種解釋：為什麼利率像是 5%、3%、7% 或落在這個範圍？他說真的有三個原因。第一是技術進步：當經濟體累積愈來愈多關於「如何做」的科學資訊，生產力會提高。所以或許那 3% 或 5%——不管它是多少——就是技術進步的速度，也就是科技改善得多快。

But that's not the only cause that von Boehm-Bawerk talked about. Another one was advantages to roundaboutness. That must be some translation from his German. But the idea is that more roundabout production is more productive. This isn't technical progress. If someone can ask you to make something directly right now, you've got to use the simplest and the most direct way to do it, if you're going to do it right now. But if you have time, you can do it in a more roundabout way. You can make tools first and do something else that makes you a more efficient producer of this. And so, maybe the interest rate is a measure of the advantages to roundaboutness.

但那不是 Boehm-Bawerk 唯一提到的成因。第二個是「迂迴生產的好處」（advantages to roundaboutness）——想必是從德文翻過來的詞。意思是：更迂迴的生產方式往往更有生產力。這不等同於技術進步。若有人要你立刻直接做出某物，你就得用當下最簡單、最直接的方法。但如果你有時間，你可以用更迂迴的方式：你可以先做工具、先做別的事，讓你之後更有效率地生產它。所以或許利率衡量的是迂迴生產的好處。

And the third cause that von Boehm-Bawerk gave is time preference. That people just prefer the present over the future. They're impatient. This is behavioral economics, I suppose. This is psychology. That, you know, you've got a box of candy sitting there. And you're looking at it and you're saying, well, I should really enjoy that next year. Well, maybe it would spoil by next year. Next month. But somehow you don't. You have an impulse to consume now. So maybe the rate of interest is the rate of time preference. Why is the interest rate 5%? It's because people are 5% happier to get something now than to get it in the future. So, he left that train of thought for us. This was not Mathematical Economics. It was Literary Economics.

他給的第三個成因是時間偏好（time preference）：人們就是偏好現在勝過未來；他們不耐煩。我想這算是行為經濟學，也是心理學。你知道，桌上放一盒糖果，你看著它，心裡說：我應該留到明年再享受。但到明年可能壞掉——那下個月好了。但不知怎地你就是不要；你有一種現在就要消費的衝動。所以或許利率就是時間偏好的比率。為什麼利率是 5%？因為人們對「現在拿到」比「未來拿到」快樂 5%。他把這條思路留給我們。這不是數理經濟學，而是文學式的經濟學。

But the next person I want to mention in the history of thought is Irving Fisher, who was a professor at Yale University. But he wrote a book in 1930 called [The] Theory of Interest. And it is the all-time classic, I think, on this topic. So, Irving Fisher is talked about in your textbook by Fabozzi, and he's talked about in many textbooks. He graduated from Yale. I mention this, since you're Yale undergrads. He was a Yale undergrad. He graduated, maybe it was in 1885.

思想史裡下一位我要提的是 Irving Fisher，他是 Yale 的教授，1930 年出版了《The Theory of Interest》。我認為那是這個主題的經典之作。你們的 Fabozzi 教科書會談到他，很多教科書也會。他畢業於 Yale——既然你們是 Yale 大學部生，我特別提這點：他是 Yale 大學部畢業，可能是在 1885 年。

And he got the first Economics Ph.D. at Yale University in the 1890's. And he just stayed here in New Haven all his life. And if you were living in New Haven in the early twentieth century, you'd know him because he was a jogger. Nobody else jogged. He would exercise and run around campus, so everyone would see him. In the 1910's or '20's, nobody did that. But he did. He was a health nut, among other things. I could talk a lot about him; he's a fascinating guy.

他在 1890 年代拿到 Yale 第一個經濟學博士，之後一輩子都待在 New Haven。如果你在二十世紀初住在 New Haven，你會認識他，因為他會慢跑——當時沒別人慢跑。他會在校園運動、跑步，所以大家都看得到他。在 1910 或 1920 年代，沒人那樣做，但他會。他除了別的事之外也是個健康狂。我可以講他很多故事；他是個迷人的人物。

I'll tell you one more story about him. He would invite students to his house for dinner. And he would explain to them before dinner that he believed that proper eating required that you chew every bite 100 times before you swallow it. So, he would tell his students to do that. And it slowed down conversation at dinner a great deal.

再講一個故事：他會邀學生到他家吃晚餐。晚餐前他會解釋：他相信正確吃法要求每一口食物咀嚼一百次再吞下去。他會要學生照做，結果晚餐對話變得非常慢。

But that's not what he's known for. What he's known for is, among other things, his theory of interest. So, this is what's talked about in your textbook and I wanted to start out with Irving Fisher because--by the way, I don't know when this room was built. Does anyone know? Because he died in '47. He probably lectured from this same blackboard, right? So, I don't know, this same slate, it could be, right? It could go back to that time. So, I'm going to put back on the board what he had on this board, I'm assuming in some time in the 1930's.

但那不是他出名的原因。他出名的事之一是他的利息理論。所以你們教科書談的就是這個，而我想從 Irving Fisher 開始——順帶一提，我不知道這間教室什麼時候蓋的，有人知道嗎？他在 1947 年去世，他很可能在這塊黑板（或同一面石板）前講過課，對吧？我不確定，但可能回溯到那個年代。所以我打算把他在 1930 年代某個時間點可能畫在黑板上的東西，再畫回來。

What your author, your textbook author, Fabozzi, emphasizes for a theory of interest is something that came from Fisher that's very simple. And he says, the interest rate--this is Fabozzi's distillation of Irving Fisher--the interest rate is the intersection of a supply and demand curve for savings. So, I'm going to put saving, s, on this axis. And on this axis, I'm going to put the interest rate, call that r. I don't know why we commonly use r for interest. It's not the first letter; it's in the middle of the word. And the idea is that there's a supply of saving at any time. That people then wish to put in the bank or someplace else to earn interest. And the theory is that the higher the interest rate, the more people will save. So, we have an upward-sloping supply curve. Now this S means supply, whereas this S down here means saving, OK?

你們教科書作者 Fabozzi 在利息理論上強調的是 Fisher 留下來、非常簡潔的一點。他說利率——這是 Fabozzi 對 Irving Fisher 的精煉——是儲蓄供需曲線的交點。所以我把儲蓄 `s` 放在這個軸，利率放在另一軸，叫它 `r`。我不知道為什麼我們常用 `r` 代表利率；那不是字首，它在字的中間。重點是：任何時點都有一股儲蓄供給，人們想把它存進銀行或別處以賺利息。理論說：利率愈高，人們愈願意儲蓄，所以我們有向上傾斜的供給曲線。注意：這裡大寫 `S` 指的是 supply（供給），而下面小寫 `s` 指的是 saving（儲蓄），好嗎？

And then there's a demand for investment capital, right? The bank lends out your saving to businesses, and the businesses want to know what the interest rate is. The lower the interest rate, the more they'll demand. So, we have a demand curve for saving. And then the intersection of the two is the interest rate. Well, it gives the interest rate on this axis and the amount of saving on the other axis. That's a very simple story. And that's what Fabozzi covers in your text.

然後還有對投資資本的需求，對吧？銀行把你們的儲蓄貸給企業，企業想知道利率是多少。利率愈低，他們需求愈多。所以我們有一條對儲蓄的需求曲線。兩者交點就是利率——一個軸給利率，另一軸給儲蓄量。故事很簡單，Fabozzi 在課本裡講的就是這個。

But I wanted to go back to another diagram that Fabozzi et al. did not include in your textbook, but it also comes from the 1930 book, [The] Theory of Interest. That is a diagram that shows a two-period story. And the thing I liked about this two-period diagram is that it brings out the von Boehm-Bawerk causes of interest rate in a very succinct way. So, this is the second Irving Fisher diagram. I'm going to do a little story telling about this.

但我想回到另一張圖：Fabozzi 等人沒把它放進你們課本，但它同樣來自 1930 年的《The Theory of Interest》。那張圖呈現兩期（two-period）的故事。我喜歡這張兩期圖，因為它很精準地把 von Boehm-Bawerk 對利率成因的想法呈現出來。這是第二張 Irving Fisher 圖。我會用一點故事來講它。

Remember the book, *Robinson Crusoe*.（課堂口誤提到 Swift；小說作者為 Daniel Defoe。）It was written in the 1700's. It was the story of a man named Robinson Crusoe, who was marooned on an island all by himself and had to live on his own with no help. This is a famous story; we call it a Robinson Crusoe economy. There's only one person in the economy, so, of course, there's no trade. But we'll move to a little bit of trade. I'm just telling you a story of the rate of--there'll be a rate of interest on Robinson Crusoe's island.

還記得《Robinson Crusoe》這本書嗎？它寫於十八世紀，講一個叫 Robinson Crusoe 的人被困在孤島上，只能靠自己、沒有外援。這故事很有名；我們稱之為 Robinson Crusoe economy（魯賓遜經濟）。經濟裡只有一個人，當然就沒有交易。但我們等等會往一點點交易移動。我只是在講一個故事：在 Robinson Crusoe 的島上，也會有一個利率。

I'm going to show here consumption today. And on this axis, consumption next year. All right. I don't remember the novel. Did anyone here read it? Somebody must have read *Robinson Crusoe*. But I'm not going to be true to the story. The story I'm going to tell is that Robinson Crusoe has some food. That's all that the whole economy just is, let's say it's grain. I don't know how he got that on the island. But he's got grain. And he's deciding how much to eat this year, and how much to plant for next year.

這裡我要放「今年的消費」，另一軸是「明年的消費」。我不記得小說細節了，在座有人讀過嗎？一定有人讀過《Robinson Crusoe》。但我不會忠於原著情節。我要講的版本是：Robinson Crusoe 有一些食物——整個經濟就只有這個，假設是穀物。我不知道他怎麼在島上弄到穀物，總之他有。他在決定今年要吃多少、要留多少明年種。

So, the total amount of grain he has is right here. So, that is his endowment of grain. That's the maximum he could eat. But if he eats it all, there won't be any grain to plant for next year, OK? So, he better not eat it all, OK? Or he'll starve next year. Now, in a simple linear production--with technology that's linear, he can choose to set aside a certain amount of grain, which is the difference between what he has and what he's consuming. And then that will produce grain next period.

所以他擁有的穀物總量就在這裡：那是他的稟賦（endowment），也是他最多能吃的量。但若他全吃掉，明年就沒有穀物可種，懂嗎？所以他最好不要全吃掉，否則明年會餓死。現在在簡單的線性生產技術下——技術是線性的——他可以選擇留下某個數量的穀物，也就是「他擁有的」減去「他消費的」差額；那些會在下一期生產出穀物。

So, I'm going to draw a straight line. That's supposed to be a straight line. These are all supposed to be straight lines here. And that is his choice set under linear technology. I'm drawing it with no decreasing returns. The idea is that for every bushel of grain that he plants, he gets two bushels next year, or whatever it is, OK? And so, if he were to consume nothing this period, he would have--if I drew this thing with the right slope of -2--he would have twice as much. This is the maximum he could have next period, OK? And so, he could consume anywhere along this point, this line. And this would be the simplest Robinson Crusoe economy.

所以我畫一條直線——它應該要是直線；這裡都應該畫成直線。在線性技術下，這是他的可行選擇集合。我畫的是沒有遞減報酬的情況。意思是：他每種下一 bushel 穀物，明年就得到兩 bushel，或類似比例，懂嗎？因此若他這一期完全不吃，若我把斜率畫成 -2 的正確比例，他明年會有兩倍那麼多：那是他下一期最多能擁有的量。所以他可以在這條線上任何一點消費。這就是最簡單的 Robinson Crusoe 經濟。

So, what does he do? Remember from elementary micro theory, he has indifference curves between consumption today and consumption tomorrow. Remember these? These are like contours of his utility. And we typically draw them like this. So, what does he do? He maximizes his utility and chooses a point with the highest indifference curve touching the production possibility frontier. This is the PPF, the production possibility frontier. And that determines the amount that he consumes and the amount that he saves. And he consumes this amount here. And the difference between his endowment and his consumption is his saving. And then, next period, he consumes this amount. All right, that's simple micro theory. That's familiar to you? So in this case, the interest rate, the slope of this line--this slope is equal to –(1+r) where r is the interest rate.

那他怎麼做？還記得初階個經：他在今天消費與明天消費之間有無差異曲線。還記得嗎？那像效用的等高線，我們通常這樣畫。他做什麼？他最大化效用，選擇最高的無差異曲線碰到生產可能邊界（PPF, production possibility frontier）的那一點。那決定了他消費多少、儲蓄多少。他在這裡消費某個量；稟賦與消費之差就是他的儲蓄；下一期他再消費另一個量。這是很基本的個經，你們熟吧？在這個案例裡，利率是這條線的斜率——斜率等於 \(-(1+r)\)，其中 \(r\) 是利率。

So in this case, I've told a very simple story. It has only one von Boehm-Bawerk cause, its roundaboutness. But maybe there's technical progress, too, I don't know. It has maybe a couple of his causes. If, as time goes by, Robinson Crusoe figures out better how to grow grain, there could be a technical progress component. But preferences don't matter in this story, right? The preferences I represented by his indifference curves. And since I've got a linear production possibility frontier, impatience doesn't matter. The interest rate in this case is decided by the technology, the slope of the curve.

在這個版本裡，故事很簡單：它基本上只有一個 Boehm-Bawerk 成因，就是迂迴性。但也許還有技術進步，我不確定；可能同時包含他講的其中幾個成因。若隨時間 Robinson Crusoe 更會種穀物，就可能出現技術進步的成分。但偏好不重要，對吧？我用無差異曲線代表偏好。而既然 PPF 是線性的，不耐煩（impatience）就不重要：此時利率由技術、由曲線斜率決定。

So, we don't have all of von Boehm-Bawerk causes yet. OK. Next step. That was the simplest Irving Fisher story. The next step is, let's suppose, however, that there are diminishing returns to investment in grain, all right? That means, for example, maybe when he grows a little bit of it he's very good at it and he produces a big crop. But as he tries to grow more grain, he gets less productive. Maybe he has to do it on the worst land or he's running out of water, or something is not going right.

所以我們還沒把 Boehm-Bawerk 的所有成因都放進來。下一步：那是 Irving Fisher 最簡單的故事。下一步假設：對穀物投資有遞減報酬（diminishing returns），好嗎？意思是，例如他一開始種一點很厲害、收成很大；但當他想種更多，生產力下降。也許他得在最差的土地上種，或水源不夠，或哪裡出問題。

Then we would change the production possibility frontier, so that it concaves down. Something like that. Do you see what I'm saying? Diminishing returns to investment. As you keep trying to add more and more grain to your production, as you save more and more, you get less and less return. So now, we have a new production possibility frontier that is more complicated.

那我們就把 PPF 改成向下凹（concave）的形狀，類似這樣。你懂我意思嗎？投資的遞減報酬：當你愈想把更多穀物加進生產、當你儲蓄愈來愈多，你得到的邊際報酬愈來愈少。現在我們有一條更複雜的新 PPF。

So now, what happens? Forget this straight line, which I drew first, and now consider a new production possibility frontier that's curved downward. Well, what does Robinson Crusoe do?
